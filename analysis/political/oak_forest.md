# Oak Forest, Illinois — LVT Political Viability Brief

**Date of analysis:** 2026-06-02
**Overall viability:** Tier 3 — Conditional
**Official alignment:** Split / uncertain (two latent city-level supporters; state legislators mostly unpositioned)
**Structural viability score:** 6/10
**Legal pathway (from brief):** Tier 5 — general statewide enabling legislation in the Illinois General Assembly before Oak Forest city council can act; city council ordinance is the final step but cannot happen first
**Model type:** split_rate:4.0 (4:1 land-to-improvement ratio, revenue-neutral city levy; practical ratio under existing constitutional cap likely 2.5:1 until constitutional amendment)

---

## 1. Summary

Oak Forest sits in an unusual position: the electoral math is among the strongest in the entire 16-city LVTShift dataset (84% of single-family homeowners would see lower bills), the new mayor explicitly wants to develop vacant parcels, the city already has an inside champion in city government, and the regional property tax grievance is acute. But the legal pathway requires state enabling legislation before the city can act — making this a multi-year effort dependent on the Illinois General Assembly, where no LVT-enabling bill has ever been introduced.

**Top opportunity:** Hunter Heyman (City of Oak Forest Community Planner, LVT fan, already engaged through the profit-loss map project) is an exceptional insider asset. He can bring the Urban3-style analysis directly to the mayor and city administrator, position Oak Forest to become the city that *asks for* state enabling legislation, and build the internal technical case that makes the policy council-ready the moment a state bill passes.

**Top risk 1:** The legal pathway is Tier 5 — the Illinois General Assembly has no LVT history, no current bill, and no identified prime sponsor. Even a sympathetic city council cannot move without state authority.

**Top risk 2:** The 4:1 modeled split-rate likely exceeds Illinois's constitutional 2.5× class differential cap, meaning a legally achievable reform — even after state enabling passes — would produce approximately a 2.5:1 ratio rather than 4:1. The directional political story (homeowners win, vacant land penalized, revenue neutral) is unchanged, but the magnitude of the shift is smaller.

**What would most change this assessment:** A single state legislator willing to introduce an enabling bill — ideally Sen. Napoleon Harris III (15th District, co-chaired the Property Tax Task Force) — would move this from Tier 3 Conditional to Tier 2 Achievable. Alternatively, Oak Forest becoming a named city in a coalition of municipalities asking SSMMA to advocate for enabling legislation would create a legitimate public process at the state level.

## 5. Political environment

### Issue salience

**Property tax burden — HIGH and regional.** Cook County Treasurer Maria Pappas documented south/southwest suburban property tax bills jumped **19.9%** in payable 2024 — the largest single-cycle increase in three decades. Pappas: "South suburban homeowners already pay some of the highest property taxes in the county, and these increases will make paying those bills even more difficult." ([Chicago Sun-Times, June 2024](https://chicago.suntimes.com/metro-state/2024/06/27/cook-county-property-tax-hikes-south-suburbs-treasurer-maria-pappas)) Oak Forest's city levy increases (3.7% TY2024, 4.9% TY2025) are modest compared to this regional surge, but the total bill increase has been felt. This grievance has no organized policy outlet — it is a wide-open political space.

**Vacant land / development — HIGH and named by officials.** Mayor Hortsman and Alderman Keeler both explicitly named vacant parcel development as a top priority in the April 2025 campaign. The 153-acre former Oak Forest Hospital campus is the dominant land policy conversation in the city — Cook County is demolishing buildings through 2028 with no announced redevelopment plan. The Gateway pads near the Metra station, the former bowling alley site, and the former mobile home park site are all named targets. Every political leader has identified the problem; none has a mechanism to solve it. LVT is that mechanism.

**Housing affordability — MODERATE.** The city has approved a 75-unit mixed-use project and a senior housing complex near the Metra station, demonstrating willingness to add housing. But Oak Forest (79.6% owner-occupied) does not have the acute displacement/affordability crisis of a dense urban area. The housing frame is less powerful here than the blight/development frame.

**Property tax bill relief — HIGH value, understated publicly.** The TY2025 levy debate generated no organized public opposition despite a 4.9% increase. But the south suburban regional context means any "here's how your bill would be lower" message would land in fertile ground with voters.

### Advocacy landscape

**South Suburban Mayors and Managers Association (SSMMA):** 45-member intergovernmental agency covering ~750,000 residents in southern Cook and Will Counties. Oak Forest is a member. SSMMA backs the **Southland Reactivation Act** — a 12-year property tax abatement for developers who revive tax-exempt commercial/industrial properties. This is structurally equivalent in goal to LVT (incentivize development of idle land). SSMMA's CEO/policy staff are the most credible coalition-building target for a state enabling campaign. ([ssmma.org](https://www.ssmma.org/)) 

**South Suburban Land Bank and Development Authority (SSLBDA):** Oak Forest is a **founding member** (along with Park Forest and Blue Island; now 40+ member suburbs). Acquires, manages, and redevelops vacant/foreclosed properties; ~200 distressed properties revitalized since inception. SSLBDA directly addresses the vacant land problem LVT addresses. Coalition partnership here is natural and immediately actionable. ([southsuburbanlandbank.org](https://www.sslbda.com/))

**Cook County Commissioner Donna Miller (6th District):** Her district includes Oak Forest and her district office is at Oak Forest City Hall (15440 S. Central Ave.). Her stated priority #1 is property tax assessment reform. Not LVT-focused (she works on valuation accuracy, not rate structure), but her network — South Suburban Action Coalition, CSEDC, SSMMA — is exactly the coalition needed for a state enabling bill. Passed a unanimous county board resolution supporting assessment data modernization. ([Cook County profile](https://www.cookcountyil.gov/all-people/donna-miller))

**Hunter Heyman (City of Oak Forest Community Planner):** Already engaged with PPI/CLE on the Urban3-style profit-loss map project. His direct message: "I work for the City of Oak Forest and would love to work with you to create a profit and loss map for the city, to assist in advocating for better land use allowances and decisions." This is the highest-value asset in the entire analysis — an inside champion with institutional standing, technical sophistication, and alignment with the LVT goal. He operates in the professional-staff layer that briefs elected officials and has access to the mayor and city administrator daily.

**Oak Forest-Crestwood Area Chamber of Commerce:** Active (200+ members). No public positions on property taxation. Potentially receptive to the commercial development argument (reduced improvement tax on new construction is pro-business). ([oc-chamber.org](https://oc-chamber.org/))

**Cook County Clergy Coalition:** December 2025, 100+ south suburban African American churches called for "immediate reforms" to the property tax system, warning recent hikes will force thousands to lose homes. Not Oak Forest-specific but represents organized moral voice for relief.

**Organized opposition identified:** None. No landlord association, homeowner association, or commercial real estate lobby has taken a position on Oak Forest property tax structure. The Illinois Association of Realtors would be the most natural organized opposition at the state level, but no Oak Forest-specific real estate lobby positions were found.

### Peer city signal

**No Illinois precedent exists.** No Illinois municipality has ever adopted split-rate or LVT-equivalent reform. Pennsylvania's 20+ cities with split-rate are the closest analog, but Pennsylvania's enabling legislation is already in place. Cook County's use-class assessment differential (residential 10% / commercial 25%) is the closest structural approximation but operates on use-class, not land/improvement. The absence of a nearby peer city is a meaningful political barrier — Illinois officials will want to "see it work somewhere first," and there is no Illinois somewhere.

**Regional adjacent signal:** The Southland Reactivation Act (SSMMA-backed, state-passed) provides partial property tax abatement for commercial redevelopment — a different mechanism that achieved state passage precisely by framing itself as development incentive rather than tax restructuring. This provides a template for how a land-incentive bill could be framed and passed in Illinois.

### Recent referenda

No property tax, housing affordability, or tax structure referenda in Oak Forest in the last 5 years were identified. Illinois does not have a tradition of municipal LVT referenda. No upcoming ballot measures were identified.

## 10. Strategic framing

**Recommended primary frame: Vacant land / development incentive**
- Directly aligned with what every Oak Forest political leader has already named as a priority
- Model data: 207 vacant parcels see +148% increase — creates an annual carrying cost for speculative land-holding
- The hospital campus, Gateway pads, and bowling alley/trailer park sites are vivid, named examples every resident knows
- Resonates with Mayor Hortsman's "fiscal conservative" identity: market incentive, not government subsidy
- Resonates with Keeler's anti-blight entrepreneurial stance
- Template: "Right now, a person who holds a vacant lot on 159th Street pays barely anything in property taxes, year after year, while doing nothing. A land value shift means every year you sit on that land is an incentive to sell it or develop it — without the city spending a dollar on subsidies."

**Recommended secondary frame: Property tax relief for homeowners**
- 84% of SFR owners see lower bills, median −$90/year (revenue neutral)
- This directly counters the "LVT is a tax increase" objection — for most residents, it's a tax cut
- Dovetails with the south suburban regional grievance (19.9% total bill increase in 2024)
- Template: "Most homeowners in Oak Forest would pay less in city property taxes — not more. The shift comes from making large vacant parcels pay their fair share."

**Frames to avoid:**
- "Land value tax" as a term — zero name recognition, association with economic theory; use "vacant land surcharge" or "development incentive tax structure" instead
- Revenue-maximization framing — this is revenue-neutral; framing it as a way to raise more money activates opposition among the fiscal conservative base (Hortsman's coalition)
- Abstract equity/redistribution framing — Oak Forest is a middle-income suburb; the winning argument is practical and local, not ideological

**Key talking points (grounded in model data):**
1. "84% of Oak Forest single-family homeowners would pay less in city property taxes — an average savings of about $90 a year — without the city collecting one dollar more or less in total."
2. "The 207 vacant parcels in Oak Forest would pay significantly more, creating a real market signal to develop or sell — exactly what the mayor and aldermen say they want."
3. "The lowest-income neighborhoods in Oak Forest benefit the most: 89% of parcels in the lowest-income block groups would see lower bills."
4. "This doesn't require a city referendum or a tax increase. It requires a change in how the tax is divided between land and buildings — revenue-neutral, same total levy, different allocation."

## 11. Open questions and research gaps

1. **Aldermen Wolf, Emmett, Vetter, McCarthy, Kunz, Stuewe:** No public record found. Before any council presentation, Hunter Heyman could map which aldermen have the most vacant parcels or the most homeowners in their ward who'd benefit — this is the parcel-level ward analysis that would make each alderman's individual electoral calculation concrete.

2. **Emil Jones III's Senate seat:** His deferred prosecution agreement runs through ~late 2026. If he faces a competitive primary in February 2026 and loses, the new senator covering Oak Forest could be more or less receptive to enabling legislation. This is a significant unknown.

3. **SSMMA staff position on LVT enabling:** SSMMA has already passed the Southland Reactivation Act through Springfield. Do they have appetite for a more ambitious property tax restructuring bill? A meeting with SSMMA CEO Kathy Straniero (or current leadership) is the highest-priority outreach step.

4. **Senate Revenue Committee Chair Villanueva's openness:** Any enabling bill must pass Villanueva's committee. No intelligence on her receptiveness to property tax innovation. She is adjacent to (but not in) Oak Forest's legislative footprint — outreach through Harris or another committee member would be needed.

5. **Precise ward-level model mapping:** The model CSV has parcel-level data. A ward-by-ward breakdown (which aldermen represent the most vacant land? the most homeowners with decreases?) would sharpen the political targeting substantially.

6. **Urban3 map delivery:** The profit-loss map project is the immediate deliverable that unlocks Hunter Heyman's ability to make the case internally. Completing and delivering that analysis is both a relationship investment and the political toolkit Oak Forest needs.

7. **2.5:1 vs. 4:1 model run:** The constitutional cap limits an achievable reform to 2.5:1 (without amendment). Running the model at 2.5:1 would show smaller but still positive SFR benefits and a smaller (but still substantial) vacant land shift — and is the honest political document for any Springfield hearing.
