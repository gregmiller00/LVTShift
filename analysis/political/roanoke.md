# Roanoke, Virginia — LVT Political Viability Brief

**Date of analysis:** 2026-06-03  
**Overall viability:** Tier 2 — Achievable (leaning toward Tier 1)  
**Official alignment:** Thin majority support (mean +0.57/7 members; four lean-yes, three unknown; blight tax unanimity provides stronger signal than individual scores suggest)  
**Structural viability score:** 9/10  
**Legal pathway (from brief):** City council ordinance under existing state law — Va. Code §58.1-3221.1 has named Roanoke since 2002. Simple majority (4 of 7) after one public hearing. No referendum required.  
**Model type (from export):** split_rate:4.0 — 4:1 land-to-improvement ratio, revenue-neutral at ~$155M (modeled); note: actual official revenue ~$95M due to institutional exemptions not captured in public GIS

---

## 1. Summary

Roanoke is politically the most favorable of the three Virginia cities analyzed in this session. The entire city council in May 2025 unanimously approved a **special penalty tax on blighted and derelict properties** — 5–10% above the standard rate for properties deemed blighted or derelict. This vote is the single most important political fact in this analysis: all seven members publicly endorsed the core principle of LVT (penalizing underutilized and neglected land) without calling it that. The bridge from that vote to a split-rate ordinance is shorter than in any other LVT-eligible Virginia city.

The model data reinforces this opportunity: **64.2% of single-family homeowners would see a tax decrease**, and Roanoke has an exceptionally high vacant land share (16.8% of taxable parcels), providing vivid blight-frame evidence for every neighborhood.

**Top 2 risks:**
1. The three council members elected on a housing-reform-skeptical platform (McGuire, Nash, Hagen) are unknowns on LVT specifically, despite supporting the blight tax. Nash and McGuire may not translate "blight penalty" support into "systematic land-value reform" support.
2. Carilion Clinic — the city's largest employer — is almost certainly a net beneficiary of LVT (their hospital facilities have enormous improvement value relative to land), but if they are also among the largest politically connected entities in Roanoke, their position (supportive, neutral, or opposed) could move swing votes.

**Top opportunity:** The blight tax framing is already on the table. LVT can be introduced as the "complete version" of the blight tax — what the council already unanimously endorsed, extended from the worst properties to all land. Mayor Cobb has explicitly linked the blight tax to Roanoke's 2040 Plan; LVT is compatible with every stated goal in that plan.

**What would most change this assessment:** An explicit public statement from any of the four lean-yes members endorsing a split-rate ordinance (likely moves this to Tier 1 Ready) — or an explicit public statement from Carilion Clinic on the policy (either direction).

## 5. Political Environment

### Issue Salience

**Blight and vacant land — VERY HIGH (and actionable).** The most politically significant recent development is the unanimous May 2025 adoption of the **blighted/derelict property special tax** (5–10% surcharge above the standard rate). This is not just a policy signal — it is a precedent. Roanoke City Council has already voted to:
1. Define a category of underutilized property that warrants a higher tax burden
2. Apply that higher burden specifically to discourage land-banking and incentivize rehabilitation
3. Frame the policy explicitly in affordable housing terms

LVT extends this exact logic from a penalty on the worst properties to a structural incentive across all land. The political work is not to introduce a new idea but to frame LVT as the complete version of what the council already endorsed.

**Housing affordability — HIGH.** Roanoke has a 18.3% poverty rate, a median household income of $55,378, and a renter rate of ~47.6%. Housing affordability is a constant council focus, as evidenced by the 2024 single-family zoning reform (eliminating single-family-only zoning) and ongoing debates. The council is clearly primed for housing policy action.

**Vacant downtown property — MODERATE.** Roanoke's downtown has experienced some commercial vacancy and development challenges typical of smaller legacy manufacturing cities. The 16.8% vacant land share suggests a citywide land-banking problem, not just a downtown issue.

**Zoning reform tension — MODERATE RISK.** The 2024 single-family zoning reform (passed on a 4-3 vote: Cobb, Volosin, Sanchez-Jones for; McGuire, Nash, Hagen against) created a political fault line on the council. Three new members were elected in part on skepticism about housing mandates. LVT is a TAX reform, not a zoning mandate — but it could be conflated with the zoning controversy by opponents. Proactive framing distinction: "This is not a zoning ordinance. It does not tell anyone what to build. It simply makes it more expensive to leave land vacant."

### Advocacy Landscape

**Commonwealth Housing Coalition** (statewide, Virginia): Supported several pro-housing bills in the 2026 Virginia General Assembly. Has a state-level presence but unclear local Roanoke chapter. No formal LVT position identified but organizational alignment is favorable.

**VOICE VA** (statewide): Faith-based housing advocacy organization that led the "Faith in Housing" campaign in the 2026 General Assembly. Demonstrated ability to bring faith congregations into housing advocacy. Roanoke has a significant faith community; VOICE VA may be an entry point for coalition-building.

**No Roanoke YIMBY chapter:** Unlike Richmond, Roanoke does not have an active YIMBY Action chapter. There is a statewide "Virginia is for YIMBYs" movement but its Roanoke presence is not documented.

**No Roanoke tenant union:** No tenant union specifically for Roanoke identified. Given the 47.6% renter rate, this is a meaningful organizational gap.

**Carilion Clinic (institutional):** The Roanoke Valley's largest employer, a major medical system, and almost certainly a large beneficiary of LVT (high improvement-to-land ratio in hospital facilities). Carilion's institutional stance on LVT is unknown and should be researched before ordinance introduction. If Carilion is legally taxable and would benefit from LVT, their support could be decisive. If they are exempt (most likely), their position is neutral on direct financial grounds but could still be politically significant.

**Virginia Realtors / Blue Ridge Association of Realtors:** Not found with a specific Roanoke LVT position, but the statewide Realtors association has opposed rent stabilization in other Virginia localities and is the most likely organized commercial opposition.

### Peer City Signal

No Virginia city has enacted split-rate yet, despite the 2002–2026 statutory authority. The 2026 HB 282 expansion (adding four more cities) represents the strongest legislative signal yet that the General Assembly considers this viable. Roanoke could be the first mover in Virginia — a significant political opportunity ("Roanoke leads the state on housing innovation") but also a risk (no local precedent to point to).

Pennsylvania provides the strongest nearby peer signal: Harrisburg, Allentown, Scranton, and ~15 other PA cities operate under split-rate taxation. These precedents can be cited, though Virginia officials typically want Virginia precedents.

### Recent Referenda

No directly relevant ballot measures in the last 5 years. The 2024 council elections (from which three new members were elected) were the closest analog — housing was a campaign theme.

### Election Calendar

Three council seats are up in November 2026 elections (all seven members serve the same 4-year term, but staggered — three seats were the November 2024 election cohort). The current council is fully **two years post-election** — the optimal policy-action window. From now through approximately October 2026 (when the next election campaign intensifies) is the maximum political runway for novel policy.

## 10. Strategic Framing

**Recommended primary frame: Blight / Vacant Land + "Complete the Work"**

The blight tax is the political opening. The primary frame for LVT should be: "The council already voted to penalize the worst land-banking. A split-rate completes that work — extending the same principle from the most egregious cases to all land, systematically." This frame:
- Requires no change in values or rhetoric from any council member
- Builds on something they already did unanimously
- Avoids conflation with the zoning controversy (it's a tax, not a mandate)
- Cites the 16.8% vacant land share as the vivid evidence ("one in six taxable parcels in Roanoke is vacant")

**Recommended secondary frame: Property Tax Relief for Homeowners**

For constituent outreach to the three new members (McGuire, Nash, Hagen), the homeowner math is the right entry point: "64% of Roanoke homeowners would pay less. The median homeowner saves about 3% on their annual tax bill. Costs shift onto vacant lots and surface parking, not onto families with homes." This frame addresses the political vulnerability of the new members' voter base without requiring them to sign onto a broad development agenda.

**Frames to avoid:**
- "This will create housing production" — connects to the zoning controversy and may activate McGuire/Hagen skepticism
- "Land value tax" as the primary label — lead with "lower improvement taxes, higher land holding costs" or "the blight tax extended system-wide"
- Income-quintile equity argument based on block-group means — the Q1 (+33.88%) and minority-concentration (+33.27%) data will be weaponized by opponents; lead with SFR medians instead

### Key Talking Points (Grounded in Model Data)

1. **"One in six taxable parcels in Roanoke is vacant. Under a split-rate, holding those empty lots becomes significantly more expensive — 153% more on average — creating an incentive to build or sell."** (Vacant land: 16.8% of taxable, median +153%)

2. **"Six in ten Roanoke homeowners would pay less under this reform. The median homeowner saves about 3% on their annual real estate tax bill."** (SFR: 64.2% decrease, median -2.95%)

3. **"Revenue-neutral. The city collects the same total amount. The bill simply shifts: vacant lots and surface parking pay more; homes and businesses pay less."** (Revenue neutrality; improvement millage decreases from 12.2 to 7.7 mills)

4. **"The council already voted that blighted land should bear a higher tax burden. A split-rate extends that principle to all land — systematically, not case-by-case."** (Blight tax precedent, May 2025 unanimous vote)

## 11. Open Questions and Research Gaps

1. **Carilion Clinic exemption and position:** Whether Carilion is legally taxable (and thus a direct financial beneficiary of LVT) or exempt (and neutral on direct financial grounds) is the single most important unresolved question. If taxable, their institutional endorsement would be decisive. If exempt, their advocacy capacity still matters. Research: request the Commissioner of the Revenue's exemption roll; contact Carilion's government affairs office directly.

2. **Top vacant land owners:** The model does not identify the largest vacant land holders by name. Identifying the top 10–20 owners of vacant parcels (by assessed land value) would enable targeted "who benefits from the current system" messaging. This data is available from the Roanoke City Assessor's office.

3. **McGuire and Hagen's specific property tax positions:** Both new members have clear housing positions (skeptical of mandates) but no documented property tax position. Research their 2024 campaign materials, voter questionnaires, and any statements in council budget hearings.

4. **Powers's record:** Evelyn Powers has no documented position found. She is a continuing member (not newly elected in 2024). Research her prior terms' tax and housing votes.

5. **Roanoke 2024 presidential election results:** The 2024 city-level result was not found in search. Biden won 62% in 2020; the 2024 margin should be verified for updated partisan lean.

6. **VOICE VA and local faith community capacity in Roanoke:** VOICE VA's national faith-housing campaign is documented at the state level. Whether a Roanoke chapter or affiliated congregation network could be mobilized for LVT advocacy is unresearched.

7. **Model revenue validation:** Official FY2025 real estate tax revenue (~$95M) vs. modeled ~$155M — gap explained by institutional exemptions not in public GIS. Obtaining the full exemption roll from the Commissioner of the Revenue would allow model recalibration and reveal who the actual stakeholders are (currently, we cannot confirm whether Carilion is taxable or exempt).
