# Richmond, Virginia — LVT Legal Brief

**Date of analysis:** 2026-06-03  
**Status:** **Open at Tier 1 — City council ordinance, no new legislation required.** Va. Code §58.1-3221.1 explicitly names Richmond as a city authorized to impose differential tax rates on land and improvements. The Virginia Constitution does not prohibit split-rate taxation; the 1998 AG Opinion confirming this led directly to the statute's enactment in 2002.  
**Recommended vehicle:** Vehicle A — direct split-rate (land millage higher than improvement millage)  
**Pathway tier:** 1 (city council ordinance under existing state authority, with mandatory public hearing only)  
**Confidence:** High — statute explicitly names Richmond; AG opinion validates constitutionality; assessment foundation is sound

---

## 1. Jurisdictional Snapshot

- **State:** Virginia  
- **City class:** Independent city — Richmond is a city of the first class under Virginia law (population >5,000; per Va. Code §15.2-1500 et seq.). As an independent city, it is not part of any county. All city government functions, including schools and general services, are funded through the city budget.  
- **Home rule / Dillon's Rule:** Virginia is a **strong Dillon's Rule state**. Localities have only those powers expressly granted by the General Assembly. Richmond cannot act beyond what state law authorizes on taxation. (See Virginia Places, "Dillon Rule in Virginia"; Va. Const. Art. VII §2.)  
- **Taxing authorities:** Richmond levies its own real estate tax. Because it is an independent city, there is no separate county levy. The adopted FY2025 rate is **$1.20 per $100 of assessed value** (12.0 mills), which funds all city operations including public schools.  
- **Current taxable base (modeled):** ~76,879 parcels; ~$43.8B taxable assessed value; theoretical gross levy ~$526M (FY2025 budget target ~$494M net after ~6.5% delinquency).  
- **Population:** ~229,000 (2020 Census); one of Virginia's five largest cities.

## 2. Constitutional Layer

### Uniformity Clause — Virginia Constitution Art. X §1

> "All property, except as hereinafter provided, shall be taxed. All taxes shall be levied and collected under general laws and shall be uniform upon the same class of subjects within the territorial limits of the authority levying the tax..."

**Pattern: PA-type permissive.** Uniformity is required *within* a class, not *across all* classes of real property. The clause does not require a single uniform rate across all real estate — it requires only that any defined class be taxed uniformly within itself.

**Classification authority** (same section):

> "The General Assembly may define and classify taxable subjects. Except as to classes of property herein expressly segregated for either State or local taxation, the General Assembly may segregate the several classes of property so as to specify and determine upon what subjects State taxes, and upon what subjects local taxes, may be levied."

This is the operative grant: the General Assembly has explicit constitutional authority to define "land" and "improvements" as separate classes. §58.1-3221.1 exercises precisely this authority.

**Rate differentiation for new municipal areas** (same section, relevant to transitional approaches):

> "The General Assembly may provide for differences in the rate of taxation to be imposed upon real estate by a city or town within all or parts of areas added to its territorial limits, or by a new unit of general government... Such differences in the rate of taxation shall bear a reasonable relationship to differences between nonrevenue-producing governmental services..."

This clause (while governing a different scenario) further confirms the constitution's openness to differential real estate taxation within a jurisdiction.

**Source:** [Va. Const. Art. X §1, Virginia Legislative Information System](https://law.lis.virginia.gov/constitution/article10/section1/)

### Assessment Foundation — Virginia Constitution Art. X §2

> "All assessments of real estate and tangible personal property shall be at their fair market value, to be ascertained as prescribed by law."

This constitutional requirement mandates 100% fair market value assessment — there is no assessment ratio, no acquisition-value lock (no Prop 13 analogue), and no partial-value assessment. Richmond's CAMA system produces separate land and improvement values at full market value, providing a sound assessment foundation for LVT administration.

**Source:** [Va. Const. Art. X §2, Virginia Legislative Information System](https://law.lis.virginia.gov/constitution/article10/section2/)

### Exemption Authority — Virginia Constitution Art. X §6

> "Except as otherwise provided in this Constitution, the following property and **no other** shall be exempt from taxation, State and local..."

The constitution then enumerates specific exempt categories: government property, churches, cemeteries, educational institutions, intangible personal property (by general law), and property used for religious/charitable/public purposes (by local designation under general law).

**Significance for Vehicle B:** The "no other" language is a hard constitutional ceiling on exemptions. A universal building exemption — treating all improvements as tax-exempt — is not within the enumerated categories and would require a **constitutional amendment** (Tier 7). This blocks Vehicle B as a pure zero-rate exemption. However, the same constraint does not apply to Vehicle A: a low (but non-zero) improvement rate is classification and rate-setting, not exemption.

**Source:** [Va. Const. Art. X §6, Virginia Legislative Information System](https://law.lis.virginia.gov/constitution/article10/section6/)

### Key Judicial / Administrative Interpretation — 1998 Attorney General Opinion

The 1998 Attorney General Opinion (Feb. 25, 1998) directly addressed split-rate local property taxation. Holding:

> "Nothing in the Constitution of Virginia (1971) prohibits the classifications you seek to make [regarding different tax rates for land versus improvements]."

However, the opinion also noted that at the time:

> "The authority to do so must be derived from the General Assembly, and, presently, there is no such authority for the different classifications you propose."

**Significance:** The AG Opinion established (a) constitutional permissibility — Art. X §1's uniformity clause does not bar split-rate taxation because uniformity applies *within* a class, and land vs. improvements are separate classes; and (b) the need for explicit statutory authority, which the General Assembly provided in 2002 by enacting §58.1-3221.1.

**Source:** [Virginia Tax — AG Opinion 02251998](https://www.tax.virginia.gov/laws-rules-decisions/attorney-generals-opinion/02251998)

## 3. Statutory Layer

### Va. Code §58.1-3221.1 — Classification of Land and Improvements for Tax Purposes

This is the load-bearing statute. In full:

**Subsection A:** "Improvements to real property are declared to be a separate class of property and shall constitute a separate classification for local taxation of real property" in the Cities of **Fairfax, Poquoson, Richmond, and Roanoke**.

**Subsection B:** The governing body of the City of Fairfax, the City of Richmond, and the City of Roanoke, "after giving public notice and an opportunity for the public to be heard" under the procedures in §58.1-3007, "may levy a tax on the property enumerated in subsection A at a different rate than the tax imposed upon the land on which it is located, provided that **the rate of tax on the property described in subsection A shall not be zero and shall not exceed the rate of tax on the land on which it is located**."

**Subsection C:** "Nothing in this section shall be construed to permit the City of Fairfax, Poquoson, Richmond, or Roanoke to alter in any way its valuation of real property covered by this section."

**Subsection D (Poquoson):** Similar authority for Poquoson without the "shall not exceed land rate" upper-bound requirement.

**Rate constraint analysis:** The statute requires that the improvement rate (a) is greater than zero and (b) does not exceed the land rate. This means:
- Land rate ≥ Improvement rate > 0
- A 4:1 split-rate (land taxed at 4× improvements) satisfies this: improvement rate = land rate ÷ 4 > 0, and improvement rate < land rate ✓
- Any split ratio where land is taxed at a higher rate than improvements is permissible

**Legislative history:**  
- 2002, c. 16 — Original enactment (Fairfax, Poquoson, Roanoke; possibly limited set)
- 2003, c. 164 — Amendment
- 2011, c. 146 — Amendment
- 2020, c. 790 — Amendment; current four-city list including Richmond confirmed

**2026 Expansion — HB 282:** Enacted 2026, signed by Governor Spanberger, effective July 1, 2026. Adds **Charlottesville, Falls Church, Fredericksburg, and Newport News** to the list of authorized cities. Passed the House 91-8. This expansion — bringing the total to eight authorized cities covering ~7% of Virginia's population — reflects broad bipartisan legislative support for the mechanism.

**Status of enactment among authorized cities:** As of June 2026, none of the four originally-authorized cities (Fairfax, Poquoson, Richmond, Roanoke) have enacted a split-rate ordinance. Richmond has conducted in-depth studies of LVT impacts, including analysis of outcomes combined with rezoning for denser development.

**Source:** [Va. Code §58.1-3221.1](https://law.lis.virginia.gov/vacode/title58.1/chapter32/section58.1-3221.1/)  
**Source:** [Bloomberg Tax — Virginia Amends Real Property Tax Classification for Certain Cities](https://news.bloombergtax.com/daily-tax-report-state/virginia-amends-real-property-tax-classification-for-certain-cities)  
**Source:** [Progress and Poverty Institute — Virginia Takes Four More Steps Towards LVT](https://progressandpovertyinstitute.org/virginia-takes-four-more-steps-towards-lvt/)

### Related Statutes

**§58.1-3007 (Public Notice Procedures):** Required procedural predicate for Richmond's split-rate ordinance. Specifies public hearing requirements before adopting tax rate changes. This is not a referendum — it is a notice-and-hearing requirement satisfied by city council process.

**§58.1-3321 (101% Rule — Reassessment Rate Reduction):** When any reassessment results in a 1%+ increase in total real property tax levied, the locality must reduce its rate to produce no more than 101% of the prior year's levy — unless it holds a *separate* public hearing to justify the higher rate. For a revenue-neutral split-rate implementation (where total levy is unchanged), this provision does not apply as a constraint since no aggregate levy increase results. Even in a non-revenue-neutral implementation, a public hearing suffices to override.

**§58.1-3221 (Rehabilitation Abatement):** Separately, Virginia allows partial improvement exemptions for rehabilitated commercial/industrial structures. This is Vehicle C territory but does not directly apply to a city-wide split-rate.

**Source:** [Va. Code §58.1-3321](https://law.lis.virginia.gov/vacode/title58.1/chapter32/section58.1-3321/)

### Pending / Recent Legislation

- **HB 282 (2026, enacted):** Expands §58.1-3221.1 to Charlottesville, Falls Church, Fredericksburg, Newport News. Effective July 1, 2026. This does not change Richmond's authority, but the legislative momentum it represents suggests further expansion could occur if desired.
- **HB 867 / SB 74 (2026):** Related companion bills with a reenactment clause, meaning they must pass again in the next legislative session to take effect. These may address additional aspects of the split-rate framework. Status: referred to committee.
- **HB 594 (2026):** Additional related bill. Status: referred to Senate Local Government Committee.

**Source:** [LegiScan — Virginia HB 282, 2026](https://legiscan.com/VA/bill/HB282/2026)

## 4. Local Authority

### Dillon's Rule and the Charter

Virginia is a strict Dillon's Rule state. Richmond's taxing authority over real property derives from state enabling statutes, not from inherent charter authority. The Richmond City Charter grants the city power to "levy and collect ad valorem taxes on real estate," but the structure of those taxes (rates, classification) is constrained by state law.

**For LVT purposes, this is resolved by the statute itself:** §58.1-3221.1 is the specific, city-named grant of split-rate authority. Richmond does not need home rule or expanded charter authority — the state has directly granted the power.

Richmond City Council can enact the split-rate ordinance under its existing taxing authority as extended by §58.1-3221.1. The only procedural requirement beyond a standard ordinance vote is the public notice and hearing under §58.1-3007.

**Source:** [Richmond City Charter, Virginia Legislative Information System](https://law.lis.virginia.gov/charters/richmond/)  
**Source:** [Virginia Places — Dillon Rule in Virginia](http://www.virginiaplaces.org/government/dillon.html)

### Richmond's LVT Study History

Richmond has conducted in-depth studies of LVT's likely impacts, particularly analyzing outcomes of combining LVT with rezoning for denser development. This represents existing analytical groundwork that reduces implementation risk. However, the city has not enacted the split-rate as of June 2026 despite having had the authority since 2020.

**Source:** [Progress and Poverty Institute — Virginia Takes Four More Steps Towards LVT](https://progressandpovertyinstitute.org/virginia-takes-four-more-steps-towards-lvt/)

## 5. Caps, Limits, and Circuit Breakers

### No Constitutional Rate Cap

Virginia has no constitutional aggregate rate cap on real property taxes (no equivalent of California's 1%, Washington's 1%, or Indiana's 1%/2%/3% cap). There is no acquisition-value assessment lock (no Prop 13 analogue). The assessment base is current fair market value.

### §58.1-3321 — The "101% Rule"

When a reassessment results in a 1%+ increase in the total real property tax levy, the locality must reduce its rate to produce no more than 101% of the prior year's levies — **unless it holds a separate public hearing** to justify a higher rate.

For a **revenue-neutral split-rate** (the LVTShift model approach), this provision is irrelevant: the total levy remains the same before and after the rate split. For any non-revenue-neutral implementation, a public hearing suffices to override the cap. This is a procedural speed bump, not an absolute limit.

**Source:** [Va. Code §58.1-3321](https://law.lis.virginia.gov/vacode/title58.1/chapter32/section58.1-3321/)

### No Voted-Levy Carve-Out Needed

Because Virginia imposes no aggregate rate cap analogous to Washington's 1% limit, there is no need to structure Richmond's LVT as a voter-approved "excess levy" to escape a cap. Vehicle E (voted-levy LVT) is thus not the necessary escape valve it is in Washington State — Vehicle A is simpler and more direct.

### Annual Levy Growth Limit

Virginia does not impose a hard statutory levy growth limit on cities comparable to Massachusetts Proposition 2½ or Washington's 101% annual growth limit. The §58.1-3321 rule operates only upon reassessment-triggered rate changes, not as an ongoing cap.

### No Circuit Breaker Refund Mechanism

Virginia does not have an Indiana-style circuit breaker that refunds property taxes above a percentage cap. This means a split-rate that raises land taxes on vacant land and parking lots will not be silently negated by a refund mechanism.

## 6. Assessment Foundation

### Separate Land and Improvement Values at Full Market Value

Virginia Constitution Art. X §2 requires assessment at "fair market value." Virginia Code requires annual reassessment for cities. Richmond's Office of the Assessor of Real Estate maintains separate `LandValue` and `DwellingValue` (improvement) assessments at the parcel level in its CAMA (Proval) system, publicly available through the ArcGIS FeatureServer (org: richmondvagis).

LVTShift model data (June 2026, 76,879 parcels):
- Median land value: $90,000
- Median improvement value: $251,000
- Improvement ratio (city-wide median): ~73.7% improvement / 26.3% land
- No default or placeholder land values observed at scale

### Reassessment Cycle

Richmond conducts annual real estate assessments, with the most recent values reflecting current market conditions. The AssessmentDate field is present at the parcel level. There is no acquisition-value lock.

### Special Assessment Regimes

**Land Use Assessment:** Virginia Code Title 58.1 Chapter 32 Article 4 authorizes use-value assessment for agricultural, forestal, horticultural, and open-space land. Richmond, as an urban independent city, has essentially no agricultural parcels. This regime does not meaningfully distort LVT administration in Richmond.

**Maggie Walker Community Land Trust (MWCLT):** 72 parcels are classified as `PropertyClassID = 750, R Ground Lease - MWCLT`. In this structure, the community land trust holds the land and leases it to residents who own the building. Under LVT, the MWCLT as land owner would bear higher land tax burden — a policy consideration for the city, but not a legal barrier.

**§58.1-3221.1(C) — No Valuation Alteration:** The statute explicitly prohibits Richmond from altering property valuations "in any way" as part of implementing the split-rate. The split-rate applies to existing CAMA-assessed values; strategic land value uplifts are prohibited.

## 9. Recommended Pathway and Legal Chain of Thought

### Recommended Vehicle and Tier

**Vehicle A (direct split-rate) at Tier 1.** The city council can enact a split-rate ordinance by majority vote after a mandatory public hearing.

### Legal Chain of Thought

1. **Constitutional permission established:** Virginia Constitution Art. X §1 requires taxes to be "uniform upon the same class of subjects." Land and improvements are *separate classes* — therefore a different rate on each is constitutionally uniform within its class. The 1998 AG Opinion explicitly confirmed: "Nothing in the Constitution of Virginia (1971) prohibits the classifications you seek to make."

2. **Statutory authority granted:** The 1998 AG Opinion identified the missing link — statutory enabling authority. The General Assembly responded in 2002 by enacting §58.1-3221.1, exercising its constitutional classification power (Art. X §1: "The General Assembly may define and classify taxable subjects") to declare improvements "a separate class of property" in specified cities. Richmond was added by the 2020 amendment (c. 790).

3. **Rate constraint satisfied:** §58.1-3221.1 requires the improvement rate to be (a) greater than zero and (b) not exceeding the land rate. A 4:1 split satisfies both: improvement rate = land rate ÷ 4, which is > 0 and ≤ land rate. The statute does not specify a maximum split ratio — any ratio where land rate ≥ improvement rate > 0 is permissible.

4. **Procedure:** Before levying the differential rate, Richmond must comply with §58.1-3007's public notice and hearing requirements. This is a standard legislative-process requirement satisfied by the city council's existing procedures; it is not a referendum.

5. **Valuation constraint:** §58.1-3221.1(C) prohibits altering property valuations as part of the split-rate implementation. The split applies to existing CAMA values — land and improvement values remain as assessed. The LVTShift model correctly uses existing assessment data without adjustment.

6. **Economic outcome:** A revenue-neutral 4:1 split-rate in Richmond would shift ~$526M in levy onto land value and away from improvement value. The LVTShift model (June 2026) shows: single-family residential median tax change -0.5%; vacant land +119%; surface parking lots +114%; large apartment buildings -19%. Total levy unchanged.

### Sequence of Actions

1. City council directs preparation of a split-rate ordinance specifying the land millage rate and improvement millage rate (subject to ratio constraint)
2. City Assessor confirms split is applied to existing CAMA assessments without alteration
3. City publishes required public notice under §58.1-3007 (at least one public hearing)
4. City council adopts ordinance by majority vote
5. Split-rate applies to next property tax billing cycle

*No state legislation, referendum, or constitutional change is required.*

### Fallback Vehicles (in order)

1. **Vehicle H (transitional phase-in):** Same Tier 1 mechanism but political risk is reduced by starting with a modest split (e.g., 1.2:1) and widening over 5–10 budget cycles. Useful if a sudden 4:1 shift generates political opposition.
2. **Vehicle C (partial improvement abatement, formal):** If the rate-based approach faces legal challenge, a formal improvement abatement program could achieve similar economics, but would likely require Tier 4–5 enabling legislation.
3. **Vehicle F (TIF districts):** Land value capture in specific development zones. Tier 1, but limited in geographic scope.

### Note on Reversibility Risk

Statutory authority can be repealed. The Amsterdam NY (1993 → repealed) and Altoona PA (2011 → reverted 2016) precedents illustrate this risk. Richmond's split-rate, once enacted, would rest on §58.1-3221.1 — a state statute that the General Assembly could amend or repeal. However, the strong bipartisan support for HB 282 (91-8 vote, veto-proof majority) and the expansion to eight cities suggests the legislative environment is favorable, not hostile. The political risk of repeal is lower than in states where the authority was more contested.

## 10. Creative Pathways Considered

### Could Vehicle B (universal building exemption) be structured as a non-zero rate approaching zero?

§58.1-3221.1 requires the improvement rate to be "not zero." A rate of $0.01/$100 on improvements is technically non-zero but functionally approaches a full land tax. This raises the question: is a de minimis non-zero rate permissible? The statute's language does not specify a minimum above zero, so this creative reading is textually supportable. However, the closer to zero the improvement rate, the more the economic outcome resembles Vehicle B — and the more likely a court would scrutinize whether the "not zero" requirement was being honored in substance. **This approach is legally risky but not clearly prohibited.** A safer path is Vehicle A at a meaningful 4:1 ratio.

### Could Richmond use §58.1-3221.1 to exempt the *first* $X of improvement value?

The statute authorizes a "different rate" on improvements — it does not explicitly authorize a dollar-amount exemption floor below which improvements are not taxed at all. A full-rate on low-improvement-value parcels with a discount on high-improvement-value parcels would be a non-uniform improvement rate — potentially violating the statute's uniformity requirement on the improvement class. **This approach is blocked by the statute's implicit uniformity-within-class requirement.**

### Could a TIF district be structured to capture land value appreciation?

Yes. Virginia's TIF authority (§58.1-3245 et seq.) allows capturing the increment in tax revenue above a base year. While not a split-rate, a TIF district that freezes the tax base on improvements while capturing land value growth achieves a partial LVT-like effect in specific zones. This is a Tier 1 vehicle for targeted application but not a city-wide LVT substitute.

## 11. Open Questions and Required Follow-up Research

1. **Exact year Richmond was added to §58.1-3221.1:** The statute's amendment history (2002, 2003, 2011, 2020) is known, but it is unverified which specific amendment added Richmond. The 2020 amendment (c. 790) is the most recent and likely candidate. Pull the enrolled bill text for each chapter citation to confirm.

2. **HB 867 / SB 74 reenactment clause:** These companion bills passed with a reenactment clause, requiring them to pass again in the next legislative session. Confirm the subject matter of these bills and whether they would modify §58.1-3221.1 in ways relevant to Richmond.

3. **§58.1-3007 public hearing specifics:** Confirm the exact notice timeline and form of notice required under §58.1-3007 for Richmond's ordinance. This is a procedural detail that would need to be addressed in drafting the ordinance.

4. **MWCLT tax implications:** The Maggie Walker Community Land Trust model creates a situation where the land-owning trust would bear the increased land tax burden under LVT, while resident homeowners own only the building. Consult with MWCLT directly on whether a LVT ordinance would create unintended hardship for CLT residents. A carve-out or alternative assessment approach for CLT parcels may be warranted (though this would require Tier 4–5 enabling to address at the assessment level).

5. **Referendum levies in Virginia:** If Vehicle A faces unexpected political obstacles, confirm whether Virginia allows referendum-approved levy structures (analogous to WA excess levies) that would allow a land-only or land-heavy levy to be enacted by voter approval rather than council ordinance alone.

6. **Richmond City Attorney review:** Before ordinance introduction, the city attorney should formally opine on the following: (a) whether the revenue-neutral split-rate triggers §58.1-3321 (likely no); (b) the minimum public notice required under §58.1-3007; and (c) whether the city's existing assessment roll structure satisfies §58.1-3221.1(C)'s no-alteration requirement.
