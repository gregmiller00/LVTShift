# Phoenix, Arizona — LVT Legal Brief

**Date of analysis:** 2026-05-26
**Status (one line):** Direct split-rate (Vehicle A) is unlock-able at Tier 5 via general state legislation creating land/improvement classification under existing constitutional authority; the constitutional uniformity clause is permissive, but the §2 closed-list exemption rule blocks Vehicles B, C, and H absent a Tier 7 amendment.
**Recommended vehicle:** Vehicle A — direct split-rate via statutory addition of land and improvement classes to ARS Title 42 Chapter 12. Vehicle E (voted-levy LVT) is a viable structural overlay to defeat the §18 residential cap and §19 levy growth cap.
**Pathway tier:** Tier 5 (general state enabling legislation), optionally layered with Tier 6 (state enabling + local referendum) to pierce constitutional rate/growth caps.
**Confidence:** Medium-High on the constitutional analysis (multiple direct primary-source citations); Medium on the political feasibility of the recommended pathway (no pending Arizona LVT bill as of May 2026, and Arizona's anti-tax political culture is more hostile than Washington's or New York's).

---

## 1. Jurisdictional snapshot

- **State:** Arizona.
- **City class:** Phoenix is a **charter city** under [Arizona Constitution Art. XIII §2](https://www.azleg.gov/const/arizona_constitution.pdf), which permits cities with population over 3,500 to "frame a charter for its own government consistent with, and subject to, the Constitution and the laws of the state." Charter authority is broad over municipal affairs but **expressly subordinated to state law**, and Arizona courts have generally treated property taxation as a state matter regardless of charter status.
- **County:** Maricopa County (non-charter; not eligible for the Art. XII §7(C) charter-county taxing override).
- **Taxing authorities on a Phoenix parcel:** the City of Phoenix (primary + secondary), Maricopa County (primary + flood, library, etc.), local school district (primary M&O + secondary bonds + state equalization), Maricopa County Community College District, and possibly special districts (CFDs, fire districts, sanitary districts).
- **Population:** ~1.65 million (largest city in Arizona, 5th largest in the U.S.).
- **Current Phoenix primary property tax rate (FY2025):** ~$1.2658 per $100 of assessed valuation, per City of Phoenix budget documents. The city historically operates near a self-imposed charter cap of $1.00/$100 of assessed valuation on the primary rate; secondary (bonded) levies are layered above. Note that "assessed valuation" is FCV × assessment ratio (10% Class 3 / 15.5% Class 1 in 2026), so the effective rate on full cash value is roughly an order of magnitude smaller than the stated millage.

## 2. Constitutional layer (Layer 1)

### 2.1 Uniformity clause — Art. IX §1

Verbatim text:

> "The power of taxation shall never be surrendered, suspended or contracted away. Except as provided by section 18 of this article, all taxes shall be uniform upon the same class of property within the territorial limits of the authority levying the tax, and shall be levied and collected for public purposes only."

This is the **Pennsylvania-permissive pattern** — uniformity is required "upon the same class of property," not across all real property. The Arizona Supreme Court has consistently interpreted this to mean the legislature **may classify** property and apply differential rates or assessment ratios across classes, so long as classification is reasonable and properties within a class are treated uniformly.

### 2.2 Closed-list exemption authority — Art. IX §2

Verbatim text of the operative closing subsection:

> "(13) All property in the state not exempt under the laws of the United States or under this constitution or exempt by law under the provisions of this section shall be subject to taxation to be ascertained as provided by law."

Section 2 enumerates the exemption categories (federal/state/county/municipal property, qualifying educational/charitable/religious property, public debts, household goods, manufacturer/wholesaler inventory, agricultural and trade/business personal property up to a statutory limit, cemeteries, veterans, widows/widowers, disabled persons). The Arizona Supreme Court and Court of Appeals have read §2 as **exclusive** — the legislature has no general power to exempt new categories of property by statute. See *University Medical Center v. Department of Revenue*, 201 Ariz. 447, 36 P.3d 1217 (App. 2001) ("the constitutional provision authorizing legislative exemptions from taxation for property of charitable institutions that is not used or held for profit controls"); *Circle K Stores, Inc. v. Apache County*, 199 Ariz. 402, 18 P.3d 713 (App. 2001) (constitutionally authorized exemption is "a specific exception to the uniformity clause"). 

**This is the single most important constitutional finding in this brief.** It distinguishes Arizona sharply from Washington (where Art. VII §1 grants the legislature a general exemption power and thereby opens Vehicle B). In Arizona, the legislature **cannot exempt "improvements" or "buildings" without amending Article IX §2**.

### 2.3 Classification authority — Art. IX §11 and judicial gloss

Verbatim text of §11:

> "From and after December 31, 1973, the manner, method and mode of assessing, equalizing and levying taxes in the state of Arizona shall be such as is prescribed by law."

This — combined with §1's permissive uniformity language — gives the legislature broad classification and valuation discretion. The seminal case is *Apache County v. Atchison, Topeka & Santa Fe Railway Co.*, 106 Ariz. 356, 476 P.2d 657 (1970), which upheld a four-class statutory regime with differential assessment ratios (60% / 40% / 25% / 18%) and ruled:

> "Railroads differ in so many respects from other industries that they may, as a class, be taxed differently or additionally."

The classification standard from *Brophy v. Powell*, 58 Ariz. 543, 121 P.2d 647 (1942): "Property may be classified by the legislature for the purpose of taxation if the ground of classification is reasonable and its results do not violate constitutional provisions."

Refining cases:
- *In re America West Airlines, Inc. v. Department of Revenue*, 179 Ariz. 528, 880 P.2d 1074 (1994): violation where "the virtually identical property of competitors in the same industry was being taxed differently." Did **not** overrule *Apache County* — confirmed that classification by industry/use remains permissible.
- *Citizens Telecommunications Co. of White Mountain v. Arizona Department of Revenue*, 206 Ariz. 33, 75 P.3d 123 (App. 2003): four-factor test for whether properties are "in the same class" — (1) direct competitors, (2) using the same equipment types, (3) providing identical services, (4) to the same customer base; with "physical attributes, productivity, use and purpose" as additional factors.
- *Aileen H. Char Life Interest v. Maricopa County*, 208 Ariz. 286, 93 P.3d 486 (2004): proper class consists of "those similarly situated properties possessing common attributes based on the nature of the property or on some other real difference in its use, utility or productivity" (emphasis added).

**Application to LVT:** Land and improvements have a "real difference in use, utility or productivity": land is non-reproducible and immobile while improvements are reproducible and depreciable; their economic functions diverge; and Arizona has already recognized improvements as a distinct tax category in another context (Class 9 — improvements on government-owned land, taxed at 1% assessment ratio per [ARS §42-15009](https://www.azleg.gov/ars/42/15009.htm), upheld in *Cutter Aviation Inc. v. Arizona Dep't of Revenue*, 191 Ariz. 485, 958 P.2d 1 (App. 1997)). A legislatively created "land" class versus "improvements on private land" class would have a substantively stronger basis than the FTZ classification upheld in *Bahr v. State of Arizona*, 195 Ariz. 79, 985 P.2d 564 (App. 1999).

### 2.4 Special-law prohibition — Art. IV Part 2 §19(9)

Verbatim text:

> "No local or special laws shall be enacted in any of the following cases, that is to say: … 9. Assessment and collection of taxes."

This is **dispositive against any Phoenix-specific enabling bill** of the Detroit/Amsterdam/Virginia §58.1-3221.1 type. Confirmed by *Tucson Electric Power Co. v. Arizona Dep't of Revenue*, 185 Ariz. 5, 912 P.2d 9 (App. 1995) (the "QTR case"), which struck down a tax on selected property classes precisely because "excluding all other classes of property from the QTR tax was special legislation benefiting non-class 1 and 2 properties."

**Any Arizona LVT pathway must be general statewide law** — typically structured as a state-level opt-in available to all cities/counties, not a Phoenix-only carve-out.

### 2.5 Constitutional caps relevant to LVT

#### 1% residential property tax cap — Art. IX §18(1)

Verbatim text:

> "The maximum amount of ad valorem taxes that may be collected from residential property in any tax year shall not exceed one per cent of the property's full cash value as limited by this section."

This cap is **enforced as a credit against the taxpayer's primary property tax with the excess paid by the state as 'additional state aid to education'** — see ARS §15-972 (state limitation on homeowner property taxes; additional state aid to school districts). Effect: a split-rate that raises residential effective rates above 1% of FCV would be silently backfilled by the state general fund rather than producing a real shift among homeowners. This is **the South Bend/Indiana circuit-breaker problem in Arizona form**.

#### Voted-levy escape — Art. IX §18(2)

Verbatim text:

> "The limitation provided in subsection (1) does not apply to:
> (a) Ad valorem taxes or special assessments levied to pay the principal of and interest and redemption charges on bonded indebtedness or other lawful long-term obligations issued or incurred for a specific purpose.
> (b) Ad valorem taxes or assessments levied by or for property improvement assessment districts, improvement districts and other special purpose districts other than counties, cities, towns, school districts and community college districts.
> (c) Ad valorem taxes levied pursuant to an election to exceed a budget, expenditure or tax limitation."

Three escape valves from the 1% residential cap:
1. Voter-approved bond service.
2. Special improvement / property-improvement assessment districts.
3. **Voter-approved override of a tax limitation** — the key carve-out for any voted-levy LVT structure.

#### LPV growth cap (Prop 117) — Art. IX §18(3)–(7)

Verbatim text of §18(3):

> "Except as otherwise provided by subsections (5), (6) and (7) of this section the value of real property and improvements and the value of mobile homes used for all ad valorem taxes except those specified in subsection (2) shall be the lesser of the full cash value of the property or an amount ten per cent greater than the value of property determined pursuant to this subsection for the prior year or an amount equal to the value of property determined pursuant to this subsection for the prior year plus one-fourth of the difference between such value and the full cash value of the property for current tax year, whichever is greater."

The constitutional ceiling on LPV growth is 10%, but the statutory implementation under [ARS §42-13301](https://www.azleg.gov/ars/42/13301.htm) (Limited Property Value, modified by Proposition 117 of 2012) provides:

> "The limited property value of property for property taxation purposes is the limited property value of the preceding valuation year plus five per cent of that value."

Effect: **Arizona has a Prop-13-lite acquisition-similar lock**. LPV is the base for all primary taxes (and after Prop 117, for most secondary taxes too), and it cannot grow more than 5%/year by statute, 10%/year by constitution. Rapidly appreciating land in central Phoenix may have an LPV substantially below its FCV. **For an LVT to ride on real market signals, the legislative enabling would need to direct that the LVT base is FCV, not LPV** — which the legislature is free to do, but which would surface political resistance from owners whose LPV has been suppressed.

#### 2% annual primary levy growth cap — Art. IX §19(1) and (5)

Verbatim text:

> "(1) The maximum amount of ad valorem taxes levied by any county, city, town or community college district shall not exceed an amount two per cent greater than the amount levied in the preceding year.
> …
> (5) The voters, in the manner prescribed by law, may elect to allow ad valorem taxation in excess of the limitation prescribed by this section."

Statutorily implemented in [ARS §42-17051](https://www.azleg.gov/ars/42/17051.htm), which multiplies the prior year's maximum levy by 1.02 and explicitly excludes bonded-debt levies (Article 5 levies).

**For an LVT designed to be revenue-neutral against current property tax revenue**, the 2% growth cap is not binding in the first year. But any LVT that effectively raises Phoenix's *combined* primary property tax revenue above the prior year + 2% would require either bonded-debt structure (Article 5), voter override (§19(5)), or special-district overlay (§19(2)(b)).

#### Real-estate transfer tax prohibition — Art. IX §24

Verbatim:

> "The state, any county, city, town, municipality or other political subdivision of the state, or any district created by law with authority to impose any tax, fee, stamp requirement or other assessment, shall not impose any new tax, fee, stamp requirement or other assessment, direct or indirect, on the act or privilege of selling, purchasing, granting, assigning, transferring, receiving, or otherwise conveying any interest in real property. This section does not apply to any tax, fee, or other assessment in existence on December 31, 2007."

This closes off real-estate transfer tax as an adjacent value-capture mechanism. Not strictly an LVT block, but worth noting because some Georgist value-capture proposals depend on transfer-tax mechanisms.

### 2.6 Local-assessment authority — Art. IX §6

Verbatim:

> "Incorporated cities, towns, and villages may be vested by law with power to make local improvements by special assessments, or by special taxation of property benefited. For all corporate purposes, all municipal corporations may be vested with authority to assess and collect taxes."

Permits special-assessment districts tied to specific benefited property — used today for CFDs and improvement districts. Not a vehicle for general LVT.

## 3. Statutory layer (Layer 2)

### 3.1 Property classification — ARS Title 42 Chapter 12

Arizona statutorily classifies all property into nine legal classes by USE, not by land/improvement status:

| Class | ARS § | Description | Assessment ratio (2026) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | [42-12001](https://www.azleg.gov/ars/42/12001.htm) | Mining, utilities, pipelines, telecommunications, shopping centers, golf courses, manufacturing, commercial/industrial | 15.5% (declines to 15% from 2027) per [42-15001](https://www.azleg.gov/ars/42/15001.htm) |
| 2(R) | [42-12002](https://www.azleg.gov/ars/42/12002.htm) | Agricultural land, nonprofit, conservation easement land, **and "all other real property and improvements to property"** not in other classes — i.e., vacant land | 15% |
| 2(P) | 42-12002 | Class 2 personal property | 15% |
| 2(C) | 42-12002 | Conservation-easement-burdened real property | 15% |
| 3 | [42-12003](https://www.azleg.gov/ars/42/12003.htm) | Owner-occupied primary residential (up to 10 acres on parcel, 40 if zoned/restricted) | 10% per [42-15003](https://www.azleg.gov/ars/42/15003.htm) |
| 4 | 42-12004 | Rental residential and non-owner-occupied residential | 10% |
| 5 | 42-12005 | Railroad and private car company | (calculated) |
| 6 | 42-12006 | Historic property, foreign trade zone, qualifying military reuse | 5% |
| 7 | 42-12007 | Commercial historic | 1–15% (graduated) |
| 8 | 42-12008 | Rental residential historic | (graduated) |
| 9 | [42-12009](https://www.azleg.gov/ars/42/12009.htm) | **Improvements on land owned by a government or Indian tribe** — taxed separately from the underlying tax-exempt land | 1% per [42-15009](https://www.azleg.gov/ars/42/15009.htm) |

**Key observations for LVT design:**

1. **Vacant land (Class 2(R))** is currently assessed at 15% — higher than residential Class 3/4 (10%). The structure thus already imposes a modestly higher effective rate on vacant land than on improved residential land. This is a small implicit land-value tilt but nowhere near a real LVT.
2. **Class 9 is the only existing class that already treats improvements separately from land at the parcel level.** Improvements on government-owned land are taxed at a 1% assessment ratio (the lowest possible) while the underlying government land is fully exempt. This is the most important statutory precedent in Arizona for the legal feasibility of splitting land and improvements into distinct tax classes.
3. There is **no existing statutory authority** for a city or county to apply different rates or assessment ratios to land vs. improvements on privately owned parcels. Vehicle A requires statutory amendment.

### 3.2 Limited Property Value (Prop 117) — ARS §42-13301

> "The limited property value of property for property taxation purposes is the limited property value of the preceding valuation year plus five per cent of that value." (ARS §42-13301(A))
> "The current limited property value of a parcel of property shall not exceed its current full cash value." (ARS §42-13301(B))

LPV is the base for all primary property taxes and (after Prop 117) for most secondary taxes. **Land and improvements are already assessed separately at full cash value** by county assessors (Maricopa County, for Phoenix), but the LPV cap may suppress land's contribution to the tax base where appreciation has been rapid.

### 3.3 Primary levy limit — ARS §42-17051

The 2% annual growth cap on the primary property tax levy is fully implemented at the statutory level. Bonded-debt levies (Article 5 of Chapter 17) are excluded. Voter-approved levies under §19(5) of the constitution would similarly be excluded.

### 3.4 Truth-in-taxation — ARS §42-17107

Cities and counties proposing to levy primary taxes above the truth-in-taxation rate (the rate that would generate the same primary tax dollars as the prior year on the same properties) must hold a public hearing. This is a procedural requirement, not a substantive cap, but it constrains the political optics of any rate increase.

### 3.5 Homeowner Rebate / Additional State Aid — ARS §15-972

The Homeowner Rebate (HOR) automatically pays 50% of a homeowner's primary school district tax (subject to a statutory ceiling), and additional state aid to education backfills any Class 3 residential parcel where the *combined* primary rate would exceed 1% of FCV. Effect: an LVT shift raising residential effective rates would be partially absorbed by state general fund rather than producing a real homeowner-to-homeowner redistribution.

### 3.6 Pending legislation

As of May 2026, **no pending Arizona bill explicitly authorizes land value taxation or land/improvement split-rate**. Recent property-tax legislation has focused on:
- SB 1224 (2024) — expanded Rule B for setting limited value on properties losing valuation protection.
- SB 1749 (2024) — increased personal property exemption to $500,000.
- HB 2315 (2023) and related — homeowner rebate adjustments.

A bespoke LVT enabling act would be a novel ask in the Arizona Legislature, which is broadly anti-tax and dominated by Republicans hostile to property tax expansion.

## 4. Local authority (Layer 3)

### 4.1 Phoenix charter

Phoenix operates under a 1913 charter (last comprehensively amended numerous times) adopted under [Arizona Constitution Art. XIII §2](https://www.azleg.gov/const/arizona_constitution.pdf). The charter is **subject to** state law. Phoenix City Charter Chapter XVIII §7 ("Annual tax levy") requires the City Council to "adopt a rate of taxation upon each one hundred dollars of valuation sufficient to raise the amount estimated to be required in the annual budget, less amounts estimated to be received from other sources." The historic charter cap of $1.00 per $100 of assessed valuation on the primary rate (excluding bonds, libraries, parks) is a self-imposed limit and not the state law cap.

Phoenix City Code §2-20 ("County officers to assess and collect all real and personal property taxes") delegates assessment and collection to Maricopa County under the state property tax regime — Phoenix does not independently assess. This means **any LVT shift would have to be implemented administratively through Maricopa County's existing assessor and treasurer infrastructure**, not by a parallel Phoenix-only assessment process.

### 4.2 Prior reform history

Arizona's last comprehensive property tax reform was the 1967 four-year-study restructure described in *Apache County v. Atchison* (placing all real and personal property into four classes with differential assessment ratios — the precursor to today's nine-class system). The 2012 Prop 117 was a major LPV restructuring but did not touch classification. Phoenix has **no public record of any prior LVT, split-rate, or land/improvement classification proposal** at city or state level (as of May 2026 research). This makes Phoenix a "blank slate" politically — no entrenched coalition for or against — but also means there is no Amsterdam-NY-1993 or Detroit-2023 precedent to point to in-state.

## 5. Caps, limits, circuit breakers (Layer 4)

Summary table of every numerical limit relevant to LVT design in Phoenix:

| Limit | Source | Operative figure | Voted/excess override? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Residential 1% cap | Art. IX §18(1) | 1% of FCV on Class 3 residential | Yes — §18(2)(a) bonds, §18(2)(b) special districts, §18(2)(c) tax-limit override |
| LPV annual growth | ARS §42-13301; Art. IX §18(3) | 5% (statutory), 10% (constitutional ceiling) | No — applies to all valuations except bond/special district ad valorem |
| Primary levy 2% growth | Art. IX §19(1); ARS §42-17051 | 2% above prior year levy | Yes — §19(5) voter override |
| Truth-in-taxation hearing | ARS §42-17107 | Procedural | n/a |
| Phoenix charter primary rate cap | Phoenix Charter ch. XVIII | ~$1.00/$100 assessed value | Self-imposed; bonds excluded |
| State revenue cap (state-level) | Art. IX §17 | 7% of total personal income | 2/3 legislative override |
| County/city expenditure limit | Art. IX §20 | Based on 1979-80 base + cost-of-living + population | §20(2)(c) voter override; §20(9) alternative limit |
| Real estate transfer tax | Art. IX §24 | Prohibited (new) | None — would require constitutional amendment |
| Homeowner Rebate / additional state aid | ARS §15-972 | Backfills primary tax above 1% of FCV on Class 3 | n/a — operates as state backstop |

The **decisive cap for LVT design** is the 1% residential cap with state-aid backfill. Any LVT that raises residential effective rates above 1% of FCV does not actually shift the tax burden to homeowners — it shifts it to the state general fund. To produce a real homeowner-to-homeowner shift on Class 3 property, the LVT must either:
- Stay below 1% of FCV on Class 3, or
- Be structured under §18(2)(c) as a voted tax-limit override (Vehicle E), or
- Use §18(2)(a) bonded-debt structure to fund the LVT-driven revenue, or
- Be paired with a constitutional amendment carving out an LVT exception from §18.

For commercial (Class 1) and rental/non-owner-occupied residential (Class 4) properties, the 1% cap does not directly apply, and an LVT shift would have full bite.

## 6. Assessment foundation (Layer 5)

- **Separate land/improvement assessment:** Yes. Maricopa County Assessor publishes parcel-level Full Cash Value (FCV) split into land and improvement components. This satisfies the basic modeling and administrative prerequisite for LVT.
- **Assessment ratio:** 10% (Class 3), 15% (Class 2 vacant), 15.5% (Class 1 commercial, declining to 15% from 2027). All applied to LPV for primary taxes.
- **Reassessment cycle:** Annual, with FCV reset to market each year (subject to the LPV growth cap for secondary purposes).
- **LPV vs FCV:** LPV grows at max 5%/year per ARS §42-13301; FCV is set annually to market. The gap between FCV and LPV is large in rapidly appreciating Maricopa County submarkets. An LVT base of LPV would be politically gentler; an LVT base of FCV would more closely track underlying land economics.
- **Use-value / special regimes:** Agricultural land (Class 2(R)) is valued under a use-value methodology (ARS §42-13101 et seq.) rather than full market value, which depresses ag-land contribution. Conservation-easement land (Class 2(C)) similarly. Historic property (Class 6) and golf-course (Class 1 statutory valuation) have administrative valuation rules.
- **Senior valuation freeze:** Art. IX §18(7) "Property Valuation Protection Option" — homeowners 65+ with income below a threshold may freeze FCV at the year of application. Mild Prop-13-like effect, but opt-in and means-tested.
- **No acquisition-value lock equivalent to CA Prop 13.** Arizona's combination of annual FCV reset + LPV growth cap is closer to MI Headlee/Proposal A than to CA Prop 13. It does not categorically prevent fair LVT administration.

**Bottom line for assessment foundation:** Phoenix has a *competent* parcel-level land/improvement assessment base. Vehicle A is administrable. The main caveat is whether the LVT base should be FCV (economically correct, politically harder) or LPV (politically gentler, weaker LVT signal).

## 9. Recommended pathway and legal chain of thought

### 9.1 The chain of reasoning

**Constitutional text:** Art. IX §1 permits the legislature to classify property and impose differential rates "upon the same class of property," with uniformity enforced *within* each class. Art. IX §11 vests in the legislature the "manner, method and mode of assessing, equalizing and levying taxes." Art. IX §2 enumerates exemption categories exclusively and forecloses Vehicles B, C, H.

**Judicial interpretation:** *Apache County* (1970) and *Brophy v. Powell* (1942) establish that the legislature can classify property "for the purpose of taxation if the ground of classification is reasonable." *Aileen Char Life Interest* (2004) defines the relevant class as "similarly situated properties possessing common attributes based on the nature of the property or on some other real difference in its use, utility or productivity." Land and improvements have a real difference in their nature (non-reproducible vs. reproducible), use (locational vs. structural), utility (perpetual vs. depreciating), and productivity (rent-extracting from natural scarcity vs. produced capital). The existence of Class 9 (improvements on government-owned land, taxed at 1% per ARS §42-15009) is direct in-state precedent that the legislature *has already* treated land and improvements as separate tax classes in one context — Vehicle A merely extends the principle to private property.

**Procedural mechanism:** A general state law adding to ARS Title 42 Chapter 12:
- A new "Class 10 — Land on Private Property" with statutorily defined assessment ratio (e.g., 15% — same as current Class 2 vacant, but applied to the *land* component of all private parcels regardless of use).
- A new "Class 11 — Improvements on Private Land" with a lower assessment ratio (e.g., 5%, 2%, or 0% to approximate Vehicle B economics).
- Application rules: when a parcel is owner-occupied residential, the existing Class 3 designation is divided into Class 10 (land) and Class 11 (improvements). The 1% residential cap of Art. IX §18(1) continues to apply to the *combined* primary tax bill on a Class 3-derived parcel — meaning state aid backfill operates as today, but the *internal allocation* between land and improvement tax is changed by the assessment-ratio differential.
- Truth-in-taxation procedural compliance under ARS §42-17107.

**Economic outcome:** The differential assessment ratios produce LVT-like economics — heavier effective rates on land, lighter on improvements — without exempting improvements (which is constitutionally forbidden) and without requiring local opt-in (which would violate Art. IV pt. 2 §19(9)).

**To defeat the §18(1) cap on Class 3:** Layer with Vehicle E — a voter-approved override under §18(2)(c) at the city level. Phoenix voters approve a tax-limitation override that authorizes the city to collect taxes above 1% of FCV on Class 3 residential where the differential assessment ratio structure would otherwise produce a primary tax above the cap. This requires either: (a) a state statute structuring the override mechanism — currently the override mechanism under §18(2)(c) is general but its implementing detail is in ARS §42-17151 et seq. and would need adjustment; or (b) framing the LVT-derived levy as a special-purpose bonded levy under §18(2)(a).

### 9.2 Sequence of actions

1. **State-level statutory drafting (Tier 5).** Draft a bill amending ARS Title 42 Chapter 12 to add Class 10 (private land) and Class 11 (improvements on private land), with implementing changes to ARS §42-15001 et seq. (assessment ratios), §42-13301 (LPV calculation — possibly directing that the LVT-base portion ride on FCV rather than LPV), and §42-17107 (truth-in-taxation). The bill should be statewide opt-in by city/county action.
2. **Constitutional defense brief.** Compile an Arizona-Supreme-Court-ready memorandum citing *Apache County*, *Brophy v. Powell*, *America West Airlines*, *Aileen Char Life Interest*, and the Class 9 / *Cutter Aviation* precedent, articulating the "reasonable ground" for classifying land separately from improvements based on the economic nature of the property.
3. **Phoenix City Council resolution of support and opt-in commitment.** Following passage of the state enabling act, Phoenix City Council adopts a resolution exercising the statutory opt-in. This is the city's only required substantive action.
4. **Voter override (Tier 6, optional but recommended).** To defeat the §18(1) residential cap and produce real homeowner-to-homeowner redistribution rather than state-aid absorption, Phoenix voters approve a tax-limit override under Art. IX §18(2)(c) at a general election. Reframe the proposition as "Phoenix Property Tax Modernization" rather than as an LVT to maximize political viability.
5. **Anti-repeal entrenchment.** The bill should include findings, a severability clause, and ideally a sunset/review mechanism rather than an automatic-repeal mechanism. Compare the Amsterdam NY 1993 and Altoona PA 2011 precedents discussed in §7 of the decisioning guide — both repealed for political rather than economic reasons. Constitutional-amendment-grade durability would require Vehicle B path (Tier 7), but Tier 5 with strong findings is the realistic interim.

### 9.3 Fallback vehicles, in order of preference

1. **Vehicle E (voted-levy LVT) standing alone** — if the legislature will not pass Vehicle A's general classification authority, Phoenix could pursue a voter-approved override structured around the existing class system, with a heavy levy on Class 2 vacant land and a lighter levy on improved residential. This achieves some LVT shift without changing classification but is small-bore.
2. **Vehicle G (special assessment district)** — for a discrete geography like downtown Phoenix or a transit corridor, form an improvement district under Art. IX §6 with internal LVT-style economics. Strong precedent for districts under ARS Title 48; weak precedent for districts that operate as general LVT.
3. **Vehicle B (universal building exemption) via Tier 7 constitutional amendment** — the cleanest legal mechanism but requires statewide ballot approval, which is politically far heavier than a statutory pathway. Best pursued only if Vehicle A is stalled.

## 10. Creative pathways considered

### 10.1 Universal building exemption (Vehicle B)

**Considered and rejected.** Art. IX §2(13) limits exemptions to those enumerated in §2 plus those required by federal or constitutional law. *Univ. Med. Ctr. v. DOR* (2001) and *Circle K Stores* (2001) confirm that even *expanding* an existing constitutional exemption requires the constitutional hook. The Washington pathway (Art. VII §1's general legislative exemption power) is not available in Arizona.

### 10.2 LPV manipulation to zero out building value

**Considered and rejected.** ARS §42-13301 defines LPV as derived from FCV; it cannot be zeroed out for buildings without amending the statute, and amending the statute to set "improvements LPV = $0" would effectively be a building exemption barred by Art. IX §2. Same constitutional block via a different statutory mechanism.

### 10.3 Assessment-ratio differential within existing classes (light Vehicle A)

**Considered and recommended as the recommended pathway.** The legislature has clear authority under *Apache County* to set differential assessment ratios. Adding new "Class 10 land" and "Class 11 improvements" classes with substantially different assessment ratios achieves LVT economics through the classification rather than exemption mechanism. This is the cleanest constitutional fit.

### 10.4 Voted-levy LVT under §18(2)(c) override

**Considered and recommended as a layered overlay.** §18(2)(c) explicitly authorizes ad valorem taxes "levied pursuant to an election to exceed a budget, expenditure or tax limitation" to escape the 1% residential cap. This is Arizona's *direct functional equivalent* of Washington's voted-levy carve-out and Indiana's referendum-levy carve-out. Underexplored in LVT literature for Arizona specifically.

### 10.5 Special assessment district overlay

**Considered and limited.** Art. IX §6 and §18(2)(b) permit special districts to escape the 1% cap, but Art. IX §6 explicitly restricts the special-assessment power to "special taxation of property benefited" — i.e., the special district must be tied to specific public improvements benefiting the district's properties. A district-wide LVT not tied to a benefit nexus would be vulnerable to challenge as exceeding the §6 grant. Useful as a pilot in a redevelopment area; not a general solution.

### 10.6 City-specific enabling bill (Detroit-style)

**Considered and rejected.** Art. IV pt. 2 §19(9) categorically bars local or special laws on "assessment and collection of taxes." *Tucson Electric Power* (the QTR case) is on-point precedent for striking down a tax structure that targets specific property classes. Any LVT must be statewide general law. This is a sharp differentiation from Detroit/Michigan (where HB 4966 was Detroit-specific by population threshold) and Virginia (where §58.1-3221.1 enumerates eligible cities).

### 10.7 Real estate transfer / Henry-George-style transaction tax

**Considered and rejected.** Art. IX §24 prohibits any "new tax, fee, stamp requirement or other assessment, direct or indirect, on the act or privilege of selling, purchasing, granting, assigning, transferring, receiving, or otherwise conveying any interest in real property," with carve-outs only for taxes in existence on December 31, 2007. Closes off the transfer-tax pathway entirely.

## 11. Open questions and required follow-up research

1. **Exact text of Phoenix City Charter Chapter XVIII §7** — confirmed via web search that the charter requires the Council to "adopt a rate of taxation upon each one hundred dollars of valuation sufficient to raise the amount estimated to be required in the annual budget," but the full charter language should be pulled directly from the Phoenix municipal code (https://phoenix.municipal.codes/Charter/XVIII_Sec7) and the related provisions of Chapter II §2 (general powers), Chapter XVI §1 (revenue and taxation generally), and Chapter XVIII §§1-12 in full to confirm there is no charter-level limit incompatible with state-enabled LVT.
2. **Specific judicial interpretation of land/improvement classification as "reasonable ground."** No Arizona case directly addresses *land vs. improvements within a single parcel* as a tax classification — the closest analog is Class 9 (improvements on government-owned land), upheld in *Cutter Aviation*, but the underlying land in Class 9 is *exempt* (federal/state/county/municipal), not subject to a different rate. A novel Arizona Supreme Court determination on whether land vs. improvements on *private* parcels constitutes a permissible classification under Art. IX §1 is the central legal risk of Vehicle A. Recommend commissioning a litigation-risk memorandum from Arizona property tax counsel.
3. **Whether the 5% LPV growth cap of ARS §42-13301 applies to a new statutorily defined class.** Open statutory drafting question: if "Class 10 land" is a new statutory category, does the existing LPV machinery apply to it, or could the enabling bill direct that Class 10 is taxed on FCV directly? Material to the strength of the LVT signal.
4. **Operative mechanism of §18(2)(c) voter override.** ARS §42-17151 et seq. implement the voter override mechanism for the §19 levy growth cap; whether the same procedural framework operates for the §18(1) residential 1% cap override or requires a parallel statutory implementation is unclear from the constitutional text and should be confirmed via the Arizona Department of Revenue's Property Tax Division (Publication PROPERTY_AZPropertyTaxation.pdf would be authoritative; was 403-blocked in this research; pull via direct request to azdor.gov).
5. **Pending litigation, attorney general opinions, or Maricopa County Assessor statements** addressing any of the above. Recommend search of Westlaw / Lexis Arizona case law database for "land value tax" / "split rate" / "improvement classification" and review of all 2020-2026 attorney general opinions on property tax classification.
6. **Phoenix-specific stakeholder mapping.** Who in Phoenix municipal government, the Maricopa County Board of Supervisors, the Arizona League of Cities and Towns, the Arizona Tax Research Association, the Goldwater Institute, the Arizona Republic editorial board, and the building-trades / realtor communities would be allies or opponents of a Tier 5 / Tier 6 LVT pathway?
7. **Whether HB2315 or any other recent bill includes any provisions that incidentally bear on classification authority** — surface review showed no LVT bill, but a deeper search of the LegiScan AZ tracker should be run before any drafting work.
