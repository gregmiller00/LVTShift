# Roanoke, Virginia — LVT Legal Brief

**Date of analysis:** 2026-06-03  
**Status:** **Open at Tier 1 — City council ordinance, no new legislation required.** Va. Code §58.1-3221.1 explicitly names Roanoke as one of the original authorized cities (enacted 2002). Virginia Constitution Art. X §1 does not bar split-rate taxation; the 1998 AG Opinion confirms constitutionality.  
**Recommended vehicle:** Vehicle A — direct split-rate (land millage higher than improvement millage)  
**Pathway tier:** 1 (city council ordinance under existing state authority, with mandatory public hearing only)  
**Confidence:** High — statute explicitly names Roanoke since 2002 (original enactment); AG Opinion validates constitutionality; assessment foundation sound. Roanoke has had this authority for 24 years without acting on it.

---

## 1. Jurisdictional Snapshot

- **State:** Virginia  
- **City class:** Independent city (Roanoke City is one of Virginia's 39 independent cities; independent of Roanoke County). Population ~100,000. As an independent city, all city services including public schools are funded through the city budget.  
- **Home rule / Dillon's Rule:** Virginia is a **strict Dillon's Rule state**. Roanoke's taxing authority derives entirely from powers expressly granted by the General Assembly. For split-rate taxation, the granting statute is §58.1-3221.1.  
- **Taxing authorities:** Roanoke levies its own real estate tax. No county levy exists (independent city). Current rate: **$1.22 per $100 of assessed value = 12.2 mills**, unchanged since FY2016.  
- **Current revenue:** ~$94.7M FY2025; projected $99.8–101.9M FY2026 (news reports; official confirmation via CAFR recommended).  
- **Parcel base:** 44,441 total parcels; modeled taxable base ~42,091 parcels (note: public GIS understates exempt base — see §6).  
- **Comparison to Richmond:** Roanoke was authorized by §58.1-3221.1 in the **original 2002 enactment** (2002, c. 16), while Richmond was added by the 2020 amendment (c. 790). Roanoke has had 24 more years than Richmond to act and has not done so. The legal posture is identical; the political posture differs.

## 2. Constitutional Layer

### Uniformity Clause — Virginia Constitution Art. X §1

> "All property, except as hereinafter provided, shall be taxed. All taxes shall be levied and collected under general laws and shall be uniform upon the same class of subjects within the territorial limits of the authority levying the tax..."

**Pattern: PA-type permissive.** Uniformity applies *within* a class, not across all classes of real property. The clause does not require equal rates on all real estate — only uniform rates within any defined class.

**Classification authority** (same section):

> "The General Assembly may define and classify taxable subjects. Except as to classes of property herein expressly segregated for either State or local taxation, the General Assembly may segregate the several classes of property so as to specify and determine upon what subjects State taxes, and upon what subjects local taxes, may be levied."

The General Assembly exercised this authority in 2002 by enacting §58.1-3221.1, declaring improvements in Roanoke (and other named cities) to be a separate class of real property.

**Source:** [Va. Const. Art. X §1, Virginia Legislative Information System](https://law.lis.virginia.gov/constitution/article10/section1/)

### Assessment Foundation — Virginia Constitution Art. X §2

> "All assessments of real estate and tangible personal property shall be at their fair market value, to be ascertained as prescribed by law."

Virginia requires 100% fair market value assessment. No acquisition-value lock (no Prop 13 analogue). No assessment ratio. Roanoke's CAMA system maintains separate land (`LANDVAL1`) and improvement (`DWELLINGVA`) values at 100% FMV.

**Source:** [Va. Const. Art. X §2, Virginia Legislative Information System](https://law.lis.virginia.gov/constitution/article10/section2/)

### Exemption Authority — Virginia Constitution Art. X §6

> "Except as otherwise provided in this Constitution, the following property and **no other** shall be exempt from taxation, State and local..."

The "no other" language is a hard ceiling on exemptions — a universal building exemption (zero improvement rate) would require a constitutional amendment. This constrains Vehicle B but does not affect Vehicle A. The statute itself also prohibits a zero improvement rate (see §3).

**Source:** [Va. Const. Art. X §6, Virginia Legislative Information System](https://law.lis.virginia.gov/constitution/article10/section6/)

### 1998 Attorney General Opinion — Constitutionality Confirmed

The 1998 AG Opinion directly addressed local split-rate taxation. Holding:

> "Nothing in the Constitution of Virginia (1971) prohibits the classifications you seek to make [regarding different tax rates for land versus improvements]."

The AG also noted (at the time) that statutory authority was then absent — directly motivating the legislature to enact §58.1-3221.1 in 2002.

**Source:** [Virginia Tax — AG Opinion 02251998](https://www.tax.virginia.gov/laws-rules-decisions/attorney-generals-opinion/02251998)

## 3. Statutory Layer

### Va. Code §58.1-3221.1 — The Granting Statute

**Subsection A:** "Improvements to real property are declared to be a separate class of property and shall constitute a separate classification for local taxation of real property" in the Cities of **Fairfax, Poquoson, Richmond, and Roanoke**.

**Subsection B:** Roanoke (along with Fairfax and Richmond) "may levy a tax on the property enumerated in subsection A at a different rate than the tax imposed upon the land on which it is located, provided that **the rate of tax on the property described in subsection A shall not be zero and shall not exceed the rate of tax on the land on which it is located.**"

The public hearing procedure under §58.1-3007 is required before levying the differential rate.

**Subsection C:** "Nothing in this section shall be construed to permit the City of Fairfax, Poquoson, Richmond, or Roanoke to alter in any way its valuation of real property covered by this section."

**Rate constraint analysis:** The statute requires improvement rate to be (a) > 0 and (b) ≤ land rate. A 4:1 split satisfies both: improvement rate = land rate ÷ 4, which is > 0 and ≤ land rate. ✓

**Legislative history:**
- **2002, c. 16** — Original enactment; Roanoke is named at founding
- 2003, c. 164 — Amendment
- 2011, c. 146 — Amendment
- 2020, c. 790 — Amendment (adds Richmond)
- **2026, HB 282** — Adds Charlottesville, Falls Church, Fredericksburg, Newport News; Roanoke's authority unchanged

Roanoke is the only currently authorized Virginia city with over 100,000 population (Richmond city has ~229,000; Newport News ~185,000 becomes authorized July 1, 2026).

**Status of enactment:** None of the four originally authorized cities have enacted a split-rate ordinance as of June 2026, despite 24 years of availability for Roanoke.

**Source:** [Va. Code §58.1-3221.1](https://law.lis.virginia.gov/vacode/title58.1/chapter32/section58.1-3221.1/)

### Related Statutes

**§58.1-3007 (Public Notice):** Mandates public notice and hearing before the governing body adopts a different rate for improvements vs. land. This is a procedural requirement satisfied by city council process — not a referendum.

**§58.1-3321 (101% Rule):** When reassessment increases total levy by 1%+, the locality must reduce its rate to produce ≤101% of prior levy, unless it holds a separate public hearing to override. A revenue-neutral split-rate does not change aggregate levy, so this provision does not apply as a constraint.

**Source:** [Va. Code §58.1-3321](https://law.lis.virginia.gov/vacode/title58.1/chapter32/section58.1-3321/)

### Pending / Recent Legislation

- **HB 282 (2026, enacted):** Adds four cities to §58.1-3221.1; does not modify Roanoke's existing authority.
- **No pending bills** identified that would narrow or repeal Roanoke's authority.

**Source:** [Va. Code §58.1-3221.1](https://law.lis.virginia.gov/vacode/title58.1/chapter32/section58.1-3221.1/)

## 4. Local Authority

### Dillon's Rule and the Charter

Virginia is a strict Dillon's Rule state. Roanoke's authority to impose differential rates derives entirely from §58.1-3221.1. The Roanoke City Charter grants the city power to levy property taxes, but the structure of those taxes is determined by state statute. Since §58.1-3221.1 expressly names Roanoke and grants split-rate authority, no charter amendment is needed.

**Source:** [Roanoke City Charter, Virginia Legislative Information System](https://law.lis.virginia.gov/charters/roanoke/)  
**Source:** [Virginia Places — Dillon Rule in Virginia](http://www.virginiaplaces.org/government/dillon.html)

### Roanoke's Prior LVT History

Roanoke has been legally authorized for split-rate since 2002 — 24 years of unused authority. The Roanoke City Council has not publicly considered or voted on a split-rate ordinance during this period. In February 2025, the council began reviewing its policy on charitable property tax exemptions, suggesting interest in property tax structure, but this review has not addressed split-rate specifically.

**Source:** [Cardinal News — Roanoke City Council Reviews Tax Exemption Policy (Feb 2025)](https://cardinalnews.org/2025/02/19/roanoke-city-council-to-take-a-closer-look-at-tax-exemption-policy/)

## 5. Caps, Limits, and Circuit Breakers

### No Constitutional Rate Cap

Virginia has no constitutional aggregate rate cap on real property taxes — no equivalent of California's 1%, Washington's 1%, or Indiana's tiered caps. There is no acquisition-value assessment lock.

### §58.1-3321 — The "101% Rule"

A reassessment-triggered procedural requirement to reduce the rate if the aggregate levy would increase by 1%+, overridable by public hearing. A revenue-neutral split-rate implementation (no change in total levy) does not trigger this provision.

**Source:** [Va. Code §58.1-3321](https://law.lis.virginia.gov/vacode/title58.1/chapter32/section58.1-3321/)

### No Voted-Levy Carve-Out Needed

Virginia imposes no aggregate levy cap analogous to Washington's 1% limit that would require structuring LVT as a voted excess levy to escape. Vehicle A (direct split-rate by ordinance) is simpler.

### No Circuit Breaker Refund

Virginia has no Indiana-style circuit breaker that refunds property taxes above a percentage cap. The split-rate will not be silently negated.

## 6. Assessment Foundation

### Separate Land and Improvement Values at 100% FMV

Virginia Constitution Art. X §2 requires 100% fair market value assessment. Roanoke's assessor maintains separate `LANDVAL1` (land) and `DWELLINGVA` (improvement) values in its CAMA system, available via the public GIS FeatureServer (gis03.roanokeva.gov).

**§58.1-3221.1(C) constraint:** The statute prohibits Roanoke from "alter[ing] in any way its valuation of real property covered by this section." The split-rate applies to existing assessed values; strategic land value uplifts are prohibited.

### Assessment Gap — Institutional Non-Profit Parcels

Roanoke's public GIS does not comprehensively encode all local tax-exemption designations. Large institutional non-profits — including Carilion Clinic (the region's largest health system and largest private employer), Roanoke College, Virginia Western Community College, and various charitable organizations — appear as taxable `400-Commercial/Industrial` parcels in the GIS export. These entities are almost certainly exempt under Va. Code §§58.1-3606–3607 by local ordinance designation, but that designation is not reflected in the public parcel layer.

**LVT modeling implication:** The LVTShift model for Roanoke generates ~$155M gross levy using the public GIS data, while official revenue is ~$95M FY2025. The gap represents ~$60M in modeled-but-actually-exempt institutional property, concentrated in large Carilion and college-campus parcels. The **official assessment roll** held by the Commissioner of the Revenue contains the complete exemption list and would close this gap.

**For the reform itself, this is a secondary issue:** legally exempt parcels pay $0 under the current system and would continue to pay $0 under a split-rate. The split-rate affects only the taxable base — the gap affects revenue projection accuracy, not the legal authority to implement.

### Reassessment Cycle

Roanoke conducts annual real estate reassessments. No acquisition-value lock or multi-year lag; assessments reflect current market conditions.

**Source:** [Roanoke City Real Estate Assessor](https://www.roanokeva.gov/277/Real-Estate-Assessor) (by analogy with Va. Const. Art. X §2 requirement)

| **A. Direct split-rate** | **OPEN** | Va. Code §58.1-3221.1 (Roanoke named since 2002); Va. Const. Art. X §1 (classification authority); 1998 AG Opinion (constitutionality confirmed) | City council ordinance + §58.1-3007 public hearing. No new state legislation, no referendum. |
| **B. Universal building exemption** | **BLOCKED** | Va. Const. Art. X §6 ("no other" property exempt beyond enumerated categories); §58.1-3221.1(B) (improvement rate "shall not be zero") | Tier 7 constitutional amendment. Not recommended given Vehicle A is open. |
| **C. | **D. Classification by use** | **NOT APPLICABLE** | Virginia lacks a residential/commercial use-classification system at city level (unlike Ohio, MA, Cook County IL). §58.1-3221.1 is the only classification mechanism available. | N/A — Vehicle A is the operative classification mechanism. |
| **E. Voted-levy LVT** | **NOT NEEDED** | No aggregate rate cap exists in Virginia requiring a voted-levy structure. Vehicle A is simpler and fully open without a referendum. | Not needed — Vehicle A already available without a vote. |
| **F. Tax-increment / value capture** | **OPEN** | Virginia has TIF authority (Va. Code §58.1-3245 et seq.) for designated development zones. Not LVT city-wide but complementary for specific areas. | Tier 1 via TIF district ordinance. |

## 9. Recommended Pathway and Legal Chain of Thought

### Recommended Vehicle and Tier

**Vehicle A (direct split-rate) at Tier 1.** City council ordinance, preceded by one public hearing under §58.1-3007.

### Legal Chain of Thought

1. **Constitutional permission:** Va. Const. Art. X §1 requires uniformity "upon the same class of subjects." The 1998 AG Opinion confirmed this does not prohibit different rates on land and improvements — they are different subjects/classes. The GA has explicit constitutional authority to "define and classify taxable subjects."

2. **Statutory grant (original, 2002):** The General Assembly exercised its classification authority by enacting §58.1-3221.1 in 2002, declaring improvements in Roanoke "a separate class of property" and authorizing Roanoke to levy "a tax on the property enumerated in subsection A at a different rate than the tax imposed upon the land on which it is located." Roanoke has been named in this statute since its original enactment — not a later addition.

3. **Rate constraint satisfied:** The improvement rate must be (a) > 0 and (b) ≤ land rate. A 4:1 split gives improvement rate = land rate ÷ 4, satisfying both constraints. ✓

4. **Procedure:** Council must comply with §58.1-3007's public notice and hearing requirements — a standard legislative process requirement, not a referendum. A simple council majority following the hearing is sufficient.

5. **Valuation constraint:** §58.1-3221.1(C) prohibits altering existing assessed values as part of the split-rate implementation. The differential rate is applied to existing CAMA values.

6. **Economic outcome:** A revenue-neutral 4:1 split-rate in Roanoke shifts levy from improvement value (reduced millage) to land value (increased millage). LVTShift model results (using public GIS data, which understates the exempt base): SFR median change -3.0%; vacant land median +153%; surface parking +130%+.

### Sequence of Actions

1. City council instructs City Manager to prepare a split-rate ordinance specifying land millage and improvement millage (subject to constraint: improvement rate ≤ land rate, > 0)
2. City Assessor confirms the split is applied to existing CAMA LANDVAL1 and DWELLINGVA values without alteration
3. City publishes required public notice under §58.1-3007 (separate from budget hearing)
4. City council holds public hearing
5. City council adopts ordinance by majority vote
6. Split-rate applies to next property tax billing cycle

**No state legislation, referendum, or constitutional change is required.**

### Fallback Vehicles

1. **Vehicle H (transitional phase-in):** Same Tier 1 mechanism; start at 1.1:1 split and widen gradually. Reduces constituent shock and reversibility risk.
2. **Vehicle F (TIF districts):** Tier 1; available for targeted development zones if city-wide split-rate faces political resistance.

### Note on 24-Year Dormancy

Roanoke has had this authority since 2002 and has not acted. The legal barrier is nonexistent — the political barrier is real. The reasons for dormancy are worth researching before introducing an ordinance: staff capacity, council composition, institutional opposition (Carilion and other large institutional landowners may lobby against), or simple inertia. The February 2025 exemption policy review suggests the council is capable of engaging with property tax structure questions; the LVT conversation could be initiated in that context.

## 10. Creative Pathways Considered

### Could the exemption data gap be used as a Vehicle B argument?

No. The gap between the public GIS (which mislabels some exempt parcels as taxable) and the official assessment roll is an administrative data issue, not a legal mechanism for achieving a building exemption. Vehicle B requires a constitutional amendment regardless.

### Could Roanoke use §58.1-3221.1 to implement a de minimis improvement rate (approaching zero)?

The statute requires the improvement rate to be "not zero." A rate of, say, $0.01/$100 is textually compliant but substantively approaches a full land tax. This creative reading is textually supportable but legally risky — the closer to zero, the more a court might scrutinize whether the "not zero" requirement is being honored in substance. A conventional 4:1 or 2:1 ratio is safer.

### Could the charitable exemption review (Feb 2025) be linked to LVT?

The February 2025 review of Roanoke's charitable exemption policy is substantively separate from split-rate taxation, but it creates a political opportunity: the conversation about WHO is taxed and WHO is exempt creates a natural opening to also address HOW the taxable base is structured. A policy package that (1) tightens exemption designations to recover revenue from questionable charitable exemptions AND (2) implements split-rate to incentivize development on taxable land could be more politically coherent than either reform alone.

## 11. Open Questions and Required Follow-up Research

1. **Official FY2025 real estate tax revenue:** The ~$94.7M figure comes from news reports, not a primary budget document. The City of Roanoke CAFR or adopted budget should be verified. The Commissioner of the Revenue's office can provide the exact assessed value of the taxable base.

2. **Complete institutional exemption list:** The public GIS does not encode all local ordinance exemptions under Va. Code §58.1-3607. A request to the Commissioner of the Revenue for the full exemption roll would produce a definitive list of which institutional parcels (Carilion, Roanoke College, etc.) are legally exempt, allowing the model to be recalibrated to the actual taxable base.

3. **Carilion Clinic exemption status:** Carilion Clinic Properties LLC appears multiple times in the highest-value `400-Commercial/Industrial` parcels. Confirming their exemption status (Va. Code §58.1-3606(A)(6) or local §58.1-3607 designation) is critical for understanding who the actual reform stakeholders are. If Carilion is exempt, they are unaffected by the split-rate; if they are taxable, they are a significant beneficiary (high improvement-to-land ratio in hospital facilities).

4. **Roanoke City Charter taxation provisions:** The charter's taxing authority language should be confirmed via Municode or the LIS charter database to ensure no unexpected local constraints.

5. **Reasons for 24-year dormancy:** No public record was found of any council consideration, staff study, or advocacy campaign for split-rate in Roanoke between 2002 and 2026. Understanding the institutional reasons for this gap would be essential before introducing an ordinance.

6. **HB 867/SB 74 reenactment clause:** These 2026 companion bills, passed with a reenactment clause, relate to the broader §58.1-3221.1 framework. Confirm the subject matter and whether enactment would affect Roanoke.
