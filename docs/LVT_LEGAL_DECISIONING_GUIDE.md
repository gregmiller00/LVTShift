# LVT Legal Decisioning Guide

A framework for determining whether — and how — a city can legally implement a land value tax (LVT), split-rate property tax, or functionally equivalent reform.

This guide is **unopinionated about the answer** but **opinionated about the questions**. Two researchers using it on the same city should reach the same conclusion about which pathways are open, which are blocked, and which require what kind of legislative or constitutional action.

It is a companion to `docs/LVT_MODELING_GUIDE.md`. The modeling guide handles the question "what would the tax shift look like?" — this guide handles "is this even legal, and if not, what would have to change?"

## 1. How to use this guide

For each city CLE evaluates, produce a short legal brief (see [§6](#6-output-template-the-per-city-legal-brief)) that:

1. Walks the six legal layers in [§2](#2-conceptual-frame-six-legal-layers).
2. Identifies which vehicles in [§3](#3-the-menu-of-legal-vehicles) are available, which are blocked, and which are blocked but unlock-able with state action.
3. Places the city on the pathway hierarchy in [§4](#4-pathway-hierarchy-easiest-to-hardest) — ordinance only, ordinance + referendum, state enabling statute, or constitutional amendment.
4. Names the specific constitutional articles, statutes, and judicial decisions that drive the conclusion.

The goal is not just to answer "can this city do LVT?" but to answer "what is the cheapest legal change that opens a credible LVT pathway here?" Often the answer is creative: a state cannot pass a split-rate tax but can pass a universal building exemption, achieving the same economic outcome by a different legal route.

## 2. Conceptual frame: six legal layers

LVT legality is determined by the interaction of six layers. A pathway is open only if every layer permits it; a pathway is closed only if a layer affirmatively prohibits it. Most "blocks" are at one specific layer and can be unwound by action at that layer alone.

### Layer 1 — State constitution

The hardest layer to change, but the one that most often determines the ceiling.

**What to look for:**

- **Uniformity clause language.** Read the exact text. There are at least four common patterns:
  - "Uniform upon the same class of subjects" (PA-type) — permissive, allows classification by statute or judicial interpretation.
  - "Uniform and equal" + "in proportion to value" (TX-type) — strict, ad-valorem and uniform together create a strong barrier.
  - "All real estate shall constitute one class" (WA-type) — explicit definitional barrier to treating land and buildings as separate classes.
  - Constitutional pre-authorization for land/improvement classification (MD Declaration of Rights Art. 15-type) — the constitution itself directs the legislature to provide for "separate assessment, classification and sub-classification of land, improvements on land and personal property." This is the most permissive pattern and is rare.
- **No uniformity clause at all** (NY, IA, CT). The constraint is purely statutory; the legislature can change the rules without amendment.
- **Classification authority for the legislature.** Does the constitution let the legislature define classes of property? (OH yes, MA yes via Amendment CXII, IL only for counties >200,000, CO mostly no post-TABOR without statewide vote.)
- **Exemption power.** Does the constitution let the legislature exempt categories of property? In most states, yes — and this is the back door to LVT in strict-uniformity jurisdictions. (WA: "Such property as the legislature may by general laws provide shall be exempt from taxation.")
- **Constitutional caps and assessment rules.** Many states embed rate caps, growth caps, or acquisition-value assessment rules directly in the constitution:
  - CA Prop 13: 1% of assessed value, base year locked at acquisition, 2% annual growth.
  - WA: 1% of true and fair value for non-voted regular levies (Art. VII §2).
  - IN: 1%/2%/3% caps for homestead / other residential & ag / other (Art. 10 §1 as amended 2010).
  - MI: 50% assessment ratio plus uniformity (Art. 9 §3).
- **Whether voted levies escape constitutional caps.** This is often the most important sub-question because voted-levy LVT becomes a viable backdoor where regular-levy LVT is barred.

### Layer 2 — State statute

The middle layer, where most useful authority actually lives.

**What to look for:**

- **Has the legislature already classified land and improvements as separate classes?** PA, VA, OH, NY (via RPTL Art. 19 in some jurisdictions), MA (via Classification Amendment + GL c.40 §56) all have some version of this.
- **Is split-rate enabling legislation specific to certain municipalities?** VA §58.1-3221.1 names the cities permitted to do it (Charlottesville, Falls Church, Fairfax, Poquoson, Richmond, Roanoke). PA enables split-rate by city class (Third Class City Code, now Title 11; Second Class A; Borough Code). Maryland's §6-203 lets municipalities choose classes; Baltimore County is excluded by other statute or its charter.
- **Procedural mechanism.** Ordinance? Referendum? Both? Public hearing requirements?
- **Explicit prohibitions or carve-outs.** Some states allow classification *except* for split land/improvement rates. Some authorize it for certain city classes but exclude others. The carve-outs are where most surprises live.
- **Existing pending bills.** NY S572, S1131, S7871; MI HB 4966 / SB 567 (Detroit-specific). A pending bill signals that the current legal status quo is recognized as restrictive.

### Layer 3 — Local authority

How does municipal power flow from the state?

**What to look for:**

- **Home rule vs. Dillon's Rule.** Virginia and Maryland are Dillon's Rule states — the locality has only the powers expressly granted by the state. Pennsylvania, Texas, and Illinois have mixed regimes. New Hampshire's NLC analysis identifies 31 Dillon's Rule states and 10 home rule states, plus mixed jurisdictions.
- **Charter authority over taxation.** Home rule does not always extend to taxation — Florida is the canonical example: home rule applies to everything *except* taxation.
- **Specific municipal codes for taxing authority.** What's the actual statutory grant the city would invoke?
- **Incorporation status.** Is this an incorporated municipality, a township, a county, or a special district? Authority differs significantly. Maryland's incorporated towns (e.g., Rockville) have classification authority that unincorporated Baltimore County does not.

### Layer 4 — Caps, limits, and circuit breakers

Statutory and constitutional limits on the *level* of tax that can be imposed.

**What to look for:**

- **Aggregate rate caps.** WA 1%, CA 1%, IN 1%/2%/3% by class.
- **Annual growth caps on the levy.** MA Proposition 2½ (2.5% growth cap), WA 101% levy limit, CO TABOR.
- **Acquisition-value lock.** CA Prop 13, MI Headlee/Proposal A. These are devastating to LVT not because they ban it but because they break the *land valuation* the tax would ride on.
- **Circuit breaker credits.** IN refunds anything over the cap, so high-millage LVT in residential areas hits the cap and is silently refunded. This is the South Bend problem.
- **Whether voted levies escape the caps.** WA yes (excess levies). IN yes (referendum-approved). This is the single most important escape valve in the modern caps regime.
- **Levy lid lifts.** WA-style mechanisms to temporarily exceed the cap.
- **Reduction factors / effective-rate mechanisms.** Ohio's House Bill 920 reduction factors hold revenue constant on existing levies as values rise — this distorts how a split-rate would be calibrated.

### Layer 5 — Assessment foundation

Even where LVT is legal, it requires an assessment system that produces credible, separately-stated, market-grade land and improvement values at the parcel level. Without this, the legal authority is hollow.

**What to look for:**

- **Are land and improvement values assessed separately at full market value?** Most jurisdictions do this, but Hawaii's counties (since 2005 in Honolulu, 2014 in Kauai) collapsed land and improvement into a single "property" rate.
- **Is assessment annual, periodic, or acquisition-based?** CA's Prop 13 acquisition-value system means assessed values bear no consistent relationship to current market values; LVT cannot be fairly administered on such a base.
- **Reassessment frequency and recency.** Pennsylvania counties reassess at very different intervals; some have not reassessed in decades, producing massive uniformity violations even before any reform.
- **Public availability of parcel-level land and improvement values.** This is also a practical modeling prerequisite — if `EMVLand` and `EMVBuilding` aren't in the parcel file, modeling and political testing are both crippled.
- **Special assessment regimes.** Use-value assessment for farmland, classified forest, current-use, historic preservation. These create rate-differential effects that interact with LVT in subtle ways.

### Layer 6 — Political and procedural feasibility

Not strictly a legal layer, but inseparable from the legal pathway.

**What to look for:**

- **Voter referendum required?** State-level enabling votes (Detroit's plan), local ratification votes (Allentown 1996), constitutional amendments (PA's history of failed amendments).
- **Sunset or pilot structure.** Some pending legislation (NY S7871) authorizes a *pilot* program — useful as a political compromise but introduces uncertainty.
- **Reversibility risk.** Altoona PA shifted to land-only in 2011 and reverted in 2016; Amsterdam NY was authorized in 1993 and the authorization was repealed. Reversibility is a feature of statutory authority and a bug of political coalitions.
- **Allies and opponents already on record.** State-level homebuilder associations, realtor associations, and chamber positions matter for which vehicles are politically achievable.

## 3. The menu of legal vehicles

A jurisdiction may have several legally distinct ways to achieve the same economic shift. Always enumerate the full menu before declaring a pathway closed.

### Vehicle A — Direct split-rate

Two different millage rates: one on land, one on improvements. Land taxed at N× the improvement rate. Requires the legal authority to treat land and improvements as separate classes (or for the underlying uniformity clause to be permissive enough not to require it).

Examples: nearly 20 PA cities; Pittsburgh until 2001; VA's enumerated cities under §58.1-3221.1.

### Vehicle B — Universal building (improvement) exemption

A 100% exemption of building/improvement value. Mathematically equivalent to a pure land tax: with buildings exempt, the millage is calibrated solely against land value. Crucially, this uses the legislature's *exemption* power — which exists even in strict-uniformity states — rather than its classification power.

This is the back door in jurisdictions like Washington where "all real estate constitutes one class" but the legislature can "by general laws" exempt property.

### Vehicle C — Partial improvement exemption / abatement

A fractional (e.g., 75%) exemption of improvement value, or an abatement above a floor (e.g., first $100,000 of improvement value taxed normally, remainder exempt). Achieves split-rate-like economics without the legal predicate of separate classes.

Spokane's modeled approach (`model_stacking_improvement_exemption` in `lvt_utils.py`) uses this vehicle.

### Vehicle D — Classification shift within existing classes

Where statute permits residential vs. commercial classification but not land vs. improvement classification, push the residential factor / CIP shift toward higher tax on commercial / lower on residential. Massachusetts Proposition 2½'s CIP shift mechanism (up to ~175%) is the leading example. This is not LVT, but it captures some of the same equity goals — and may be a politically necessary stepping stone.

### Vehicle E — Voted-levy LVT

In states where voter-approved levies escape the regular rate caps and uniformity strictures (WA, IN), structure the LVT as a voted excess levy. The voted levy can fund city or county functions exclusively from land value, even where the regular levy regime cannot accommodate split rates.

This is the most under-explored vehicle and deserves particular attention in capped states.

### Vehicle F — Tax-increment / value-capture mechanisms

Not LVT, but adjacent — capture incremental land value uplift (e.g., from infrastructure investment) without changing the underlying rate structure. Useful as a transitional tool or in jurisdictions where full LVT is blocked.

### Vehicle G — Special assessment districts

A creative path in some jurisdictions: form a special district whose enabling statute permits its own classification scheme, then layer the LVT within the district. High procedural complexity but worth exploring in jurisdictions where general municipal authority is blocked.

### Vehicle H — Transitional abatements layered on a flat rate

Phase-in mechanisms: a uniform rate on the books, with declining building-value abatements over a multi-year transition. This sidesteps the classification question entirely by characterizing the differential as an exemption regime.

## 4. Pathway hierarchy: easiest to hardest

Score every candidate city on this ladder. The cheapest legal change that opens a credible pathway is the one to pursue first.

| Tier | Pathway | What it requires | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Ordinance alone** | Existing state authority + existing local home rule | Pittsburgh's historic split rate; PA cities operating under Third Class City Code |
| 2 | **Ordinance + local referendum** | Local voter ratification of existing authority | Allentown PA (1996) |
| 3 | **Opt-in to existing state statute** | Pick up an option the state already created | NY RPTL Art. 19 homestead option for eligible cities |
| 4 | **State enabling legislation (city-specific)** | A statute naming this city, no constitutional change | VA §58.1-3221.1 expansion to additional cities; MI HB 4966 for Detroit |
| 5 | **State enabling legislation (general)** | A statewide statute permitting all cities to opt in | NY S1131 (pending); a hypothetical WA universal building exemption authorizing statute |
| 6 | **State enabling + local referendum** | Statewide authority plus city-level vote | Detroit's plan (state authorization then city vote) |
| 7 | **State constitutional amendment** | The state's constitution itself blocks the pathway | WA (to amend "all real estate is one class"); PA (any classification beyond what current interpretation allows) |
| 8 | **Constitutional amendment + assessment overhaul** | The constitution both blocks the rate structure and breaks the valuation base | CA (Prop 13 lock); MI (Headlee/Proposal A) |

Some cities sit at different tiers depending on which **vehicle** they pursue. Washington State is Tier 5 (state enabling legislation for universal building exemption) for Vehicle B, but Tier 7 (constitutional amendment) for Vehicle A. The vehicle taxonomy in [§3](#3-the-menu-of-legal-vehicles) and the pathway hierarchy here are orthogonal — always score the pair.

## 5. Diagnostic intake — questions to answer for every city

For each city, walk this checklist in order. The first "no" or "blocked" answer doesn't end the analysis — it routes you to the next vehicle.

### A. Jurisdictional foundations

1. Which state is the city in?
2. Is it incorporated? Under what state code (city of which class, town, township, etc.)?
3. Is the state a home rule, Dillon's Rule, or mixed regime?
4. Does the city have charter authority over its own property tax? Or only what the state grants?
5. Is this a city, a county, a school district, or some combination as the relevant taxing authority for the levies in question?

### B. Constitutional layer (Layer 1)

6. What is the exact text of the state's uniformity clause? (Cite article and section.)
7. Has it been judicially interpreted to require equal rates across all real property? Or only within statutorily defined classes?
8. Does the constitution define real property as "one class"?
9. Does the constitution grant the legislature classification authority?
10. Does the constitution grant the legislature exemption authority? What is the exact language?
11. Are there constitutional rate caps? (Aggregate, growth, acquisition-value.)
12. Do voted/excess levies escape those caps?

### C. Statutory layer (Layer 2)

13. Has the legislature classified real property into land and improvements (or into residential/commercial/etc.)?
14. Is there an enabling statute for split-rate or universal building exemption? Cite it.
15. If yes, does it apply to *this* city's class? (Beware of carve-outs.)
16. What's the procedural mechanism — ordinance, referendum, public hearing, state approval?
17. Are there pending bills that would change the answer? Cite the bill number and current status.

### D. Local authority (Layer 3)

18. Does the municipal charter or code already permit different rates on land and improvements?
19. Are there special assessment districts or TIF mechanisms with their own classification authority?
20. Has the locality previously attempted LVT, split-rate, or classification reform? With what outcome?

### E. Caps and circuit breakers (Layer 4)

21. What aggregate rate caps apply?
22. What annual growth caps apply (on the levy, not just on the rate)?
23. Is there acquisition-value or other assessment-locking mechanism?
24. Are there circuit breaker credits that effectively refund tax above some threshold? Do they apply to voted levies?
25. What levy lid lift or excess levy mechanisms exist?

### F. Assessment foundation (Layer 5)

26. Are land and improvement values assessed separately and publicly available?
27. What is the reassessment cycle, and when was the last reassessment?
28. Are there use-value or current-use special assessment regimes that would distort LVT economics?
29. Is the assessment ratio 100% of market value, or some other percentage?
30. Are condo, mobile home, or other property types assessed in unusual ways that need correction (cf. St. Paul condo collapse)?

### G. Political and procedural feasibility (Layer 6)

31. What state-level votes (legislative, referendum) and local-level votes are required for each vehicle?
32. Is there a pilot or sunset structure available?
33. What is the historical record of LVT or classification reform in this state?
34. Who are the named statutory advocates and opponents, if any?

## 7. Worked examples

These examples illustrate how the framework applies. They are also stress tests — if the framework gives a different answer than the substantive legal analysis, the framework needs another question.

### 7.1 Philadelphia, Pennsylvania — Tier 1 (ordinance alone)

- **Constitutional layer.** PA Constitution Art. VIII §1: "All taxes shall be uniform, upon the same class of subjects, within the territorial limits of the authority levying the tax." The PA Supreme Court has interpreted "same class of subjects" to *permit* land and improvements as distinct classes for split-rate taxation, even while interpreting uniformity strictly in other contexts (graduated income tax was held to violate the clause).
- **Statutory layer.** Pennsylvania's enabling statutes (Third Class City Code, now consolidated in Title 11 of Pa.C.S.; Borough Code; First Class A City provisions for Pittsburgh; specific statutes for Philadelphia as a First Class City) all authorize split-rate.
- **Local authority.** Philadelphia operates under home rule charter.
- **Caps.** No constitutional rate cap analogous to WA or CA.
- **Assessment.** Office of Property Assessment publishes separate land and improvement values.
- **Open vehicles.** A, B, C, E all available. Vehicle A (direct split-rate) is the canonical pathway: ~20 PA cities use it, including Allentown (since 1996), Harrisburg (since 1982), Scranton, and historically Pittsburgh.
- **Recommended pathway.** Tier 1 — city council ordinance. Voter approval not required by state law but politically standard.

### 7.2 Rockville, Maryland — Tier 1 to Tier 3 (ordinance, possibly with state notice)

- **Constitutional layer.** Maryland's Declaration of Rights Art. 15 expressly contemplates separate land/improvement classification: "The General Assembly shall, by uniform rules, provide for the separate assessment, classification and sub-classification of land, improvements on land and personal property, as it may deem proper." Taxes must then be uniform *within each class or sub-class* — exactly the structure required for split-rate. Maryland is constitutionally one of the most LVT-permissive states by virtue of this provision.
- **Statutory layer.** Md. Tax-Property §6-203 grants the governing body of a municipal corporation authority to impose property tax on "those classes of property that it selects." Together with §6-304 ("No Additional Law Necessary for Special Rate") and the Title 8 classification regime, an incorporated municipality has authority to treat land and improvements as separate classes for municipal tax purposes — operating within the explicit constitutional permission of Declaration of Rights Art. 15.
- **Local authority.** Rockville is an incorporated municipality with charter authority over its property tax rate.
- **Caps.** Maryland does not impose a constitutional rate cap.
- **Assessment.** SDAT assesses statewide; land and improvement values are separately stated.
- **Open vehicles.** A, B, C available. Note that this authority is limited to the *municipal* slice of the tax bill; the *county* portion (Montgomery County for Rockville) is governed by §6-302 and is not classifiable in the same way.
- **Note on Baltimore County.** Baltimore County is a *county*, not an incorporated municipality, and is governed by §6-302 rather than §6-203. Its authority to impose differentiated rates on land and improvements is far more constrained — effectively requiring state enabling legislation specific to the county. This is a clean example of how *incorporation status* alone changes the pathway tier.
- **Recommended pathway.** Tier 1 — Rockville city council ordinance, applied to municipal levy only. To extend the shift to the county portion of the bill, Tier 4 state enabling for Montgomery County would be needed.

### 7.3 Washington State (Bellingham, Spokane, Seattle) — Tier 5 via Vehicle B; Tier 7 via Vehicle A

- **Constitutional layer.** Wash. Const. Art. VII §1: "All taxes shall be uniform upon the same class of property … All real estate shall constitute one class." This is the most explicit constitutional barrier to direct split-rate (Vehicle A) of any state. Art. VII §2 imposes the 1% cap on non-voted regular levies.
- **Exemption pathway.** Art. VII §1 also provides: "Such property as the legislature may by general laws provide shall be exempt from taxation." The legislature's exemption power is broad and has been used for many categories. A universal exemption of building value (Vehicle B) is mathematically equivalent to a land tax but legally a use of exemption authority rather than classification authority. This is the **single most important creative pathway in the guide.**
- **Voted-levy pathway.** Voter-approved excess levies (school M&O, lid lifts) escape the 1% regular-levy cap. A Vehicle E voted-levy LVT — a referendum-approved levy whose base is land value alone (via Vehicle B exemption) — could be funded without breaching the cap.
- **Statutory layer.** Implementing Vehicle B requires the legislature to enact a general law providing the building exemption, since cities cannot grant exemptions to state-uniformity-bound classes on their own.
- **Caps.** 1% aggregate cap on regular levies; 101% levy growth limit (RCW 84.55.010); voted excess levies are over-and-above.
- **Open vehicles.** B and E are the realistic pathways; A is constitutionally blocked absent amendment; C and H are arguably available with statutory authority.
- **Recommended pathway.** Tier 5 for Vehicle B — state enabling legislation creating a universal (or large partial) building exemption that cities can opt into.

### 7.4 Syracuse, New York — Tier 5

- **Constitutional layer.** New York has no constitutional uniformity clause on property tax (one of three states alongside Iowa and Connecticut). The constraint is entirely statutory.
- **Statutory layer.** Real Property Tax Law currently requires a single tax rate per assessing unit, with exceptions: RPTL Article 19 provides a Homestead Tax Option for eligible cities/towns/villages/counties/school districts that have completed a revaluation. Article 19 is *not* available in NYC or most of Nassau County.
- **Pending legislation.** S1131 (general LVT authorization for municipalities), S7871 (LVT pilot program), and historical precedent (Amsterdam authorized 1993, repealed) demonstrate the legislative pathway is the operative constraint.
- **Local authority.** Syracuse has home rule on most matters but property tax classification is preempted by RPTL.
- **Caps.** NY has a statutory tax cap (2% or CPI, whichever is less) on local government tax levy growth, with override mechanisms.
- **Assessment.** Onondaga County assesses; Syracuse has full-value assessment.
- **Open vehicles.** Vehicle D (Homestead option) is available now but only achieves residential/non-residential differentiation, not land/improvement differentiation. Vehicles A, B, C all require either S1131/S7871-class enabling legislation or city-specific authorization.
- **Recommended pathway.** Tier 5 — pass S1131 or equivalent. Alternative: Tier 4 city-specific bill for Syracuse, modeled on the 1993 Amsterdam authorization but with anti-repeal entrenchment.

### 7.5 Texas — Tier 7 (constitutional amendment)

- **Constitutional layer.** Tex. Const. Art. VIII §1: "Taxation shall be equal and uniform … All real property and tangible personal property in this State … shall be taxed in proportion to its value, which shall be ascertained as may be provided by law." The combination of "equal and uniform" with "in proportion to value" creates a strict ad-valorem requirement on a single market-value base. Carve-outs exist for agricultural and timber use-value assessment, but these are themselves constitutionally enumerated exceptions.
- **Statutory layer.** No general LVT or split-rate authority. The Texas Property Tax Code is built around the single-base ad-valorem premise.
- **Caps.** Multiple statutory caps on levy growth and rates; Senate Bill 2 (2019) added growth limits.
- **Open vehicles.** Essentially none without a constitutional amendment to permit classification of land and improvements as distinct classes, *or* a constitutional amendment carving out a building exemption analogous to the existing agricultural-use carve-out.
- **Recommended pathway.** Tier 7 — constitutional amendment. The agricultural-use precedent suggests amendment is not unimaginable but politically very heavy.

### 7.6 California — Tier 8 (constitutional amendment + assessment overhaul)

- **Constitutional layer.** Prop 13 (Art. XIII A) imposes a 1% rate cap, sets each property's base year value at acquisition, and limits annual growth in assessed value to 2%. Prop 13 also requires two-thirds supermajority votes for special taxes.
- **The assessment problem.** Even if a California city were authorized to split land and improvement rates, the underlying assessed values have no consistent relationship to current market values. A parcel acquired in 1978 and one acquired in 2024 next door have radically different assessed bases — and an LVT cannot be fairly administered on top of that. *This isn't an assessment-quality problem; it's a written constitutional rule that disqualifies parcel-level revaluation.*
- **Open vehicles.** None within Prop 13. A "split-roll" amendment (Prop 15, 2020) failed at the ballot. Any LVT in California requires both classification authority and a constitutional fix to the assessment base for the affected parcels.
- **Recommended pathway.** Tier 8. The realistic intermediate goal is split-roll reassessment for commercial property; full LVT requires another step beyond that.

### 7.7 South Bend, Indiana — Tier 4 or Tier 5, with circuit-breaker caveat

- **Constitutional layer.** Ind. Const. Art. 10 §1 (as amended 2010) caps homestead property tax at 1%, other residential and agricultural at 2%, and all other at 3% of gross assessed value. Uniformity language is present but classification by use is constitutional.
- **The circuit breaker.** When a parcel's effective tax rate exceeds its cap (e.g., 1% on homesteads), the excess is automatically refunded as a "circuit breaker credit." In South Bend, modeling shows that *most* parcels are already at or near the cap under the current millage. A split-rate that raises the effective rate on residential land would mostly translate into larger circuit breaker credits, not actual tax shift.
- **Voted levies and the cap.** Voter-approved referendum levies (under IC 6-1.1-20 referendum framework and HEA 1001 of 2018's controlled-project rules) are excluded from the circuit-breaker cap. This is the key escape valve: an LVT funded through a voted referendum levy can produce real tax shifts even where ordinary levies are circuit-breakered.
- **Statutory layer.** Indiana does not currently have general split-rate enabling legislation. State authorization would be required.
- **Open vehicles.** Vehicle E (voted-levy LVT) is the most promising, conditional on state enabling. Vehicles A–C require enabling legislation and would be largely neutralized by circuit breakers unless structured as voted levies.
- **Recommended pathway.** Tier 4–5 (state enabling), explicitly structured around the voted-levy carve-out. Confirm via Indiana Department of Local Government Finance that referendum levies are fully outside the cap.

### 7.8 Detroit, Michigan — Tier 5 + Tier 6

- **Constitutional layer.** Mich. Const. Art. 9 §3 requires uniformity and limits assessment to 50% of true cash value. Headlee Amendment (1978) and Proposal A (1994) layer growth caps on the assessed value of individual parcels.
- **Statutory layer.** No split-rate authority currently. The Duggan administration's plan required state enabling legislation introduced in 2023.
- **Open vehicles.** A and B both require state action. Concerns about the 50% assessment cap and uniformity have been raised in published critiques.
- **Recommended pathway.** Tier 5 state enabling + Tier 6 local referendum.

### 7.9 Boston / Massachusetts — operating on Vehicle D today

- **Constitutional layer.** Mass. Const. Amendment CXII (1978) authorizes the legislature to define up to four classes (residential, open space, commercial, industrial) with different rates.
- **Statutory layer.** GL c.40 §56 implements with the CIP shift mechanism up to ~175%.
- **Open vehicles.** Vehicle D operates today in 100+ municipalities. Vehicles A–C would require expanding the classification authority — likely a constitutional amendment to permit a fifth/sixth class for land vs. improvements, or a statutory addition that survives constitutional review.
- **Recommended pathway.** Vehicle D is functionally available now and captures some equity goals; for full LVT, Tier 7.

### 7.10 Cook County, Illinois — Vehicle D today, Vehicle A blocked

- **Constitutional layer.** Ill. Const. Art. IX §4 permits counties >200,000 population to classify real property; only Cook County has adopted classification.
- **Statutory and local layer.** Cook County classifies by use (residential 10%, commercial/industrial 25% assessment level), not by land vs. improvements.
- **Open vehicles.** Vehicle D operates already; Vehicle A would require state-level reinterpretation or amendment of the classification clause to include land/improvement distinctions.
- **Recommended pathway.** Tier 4–5 for general LVT enabling; Vehicle D is the existing tool.

## 8. Research methodology — where to find each piece

For each layer, the canonical sources:

**State constitution.** Justia (`law.justia.com/constitution/<state>`), Ballotpedia article-by-article pages, FindLaw constitutional indexes, and the state legislature's official constitution page. Read the entire revenue/taxation article, not just the uniformity clause — exemption power and classification authority often live nearby.

**Judicial interpretation.** State supreme court opinions on the uniformity clause. The Lincoln Institute of Land Policy and the Progress and Poverty Institute have aggregated commentary. *Aldine Apartments v. Commonwealth* (PA), *Allied Stores of Ohio v. Bowers* (OH), and the WA cases interpreting "one class" are touchstones.

**State statute.** State legislature website (`legis.state.<xx>.us` or equivalent), Justia state code indexes, FindLaw state code pages. Search for "classification of real property," "tax rate," "exemption," and the specific city class.

**Local authority and home rule status.** National League of Cities reports, Ballotpedia's home rule pages, and the state's municipal code commission. For each candidate city, also pull the actual charter.

**Caps.** Tax Foundation's "Property Tax Limitation Regimes" primer covers all 50 states. State department of revenue / property tax administration handbooks are authoritative for mechanics.

**Assessment foundation.** State department of revenue + county assessor handbooks. For Pennsylvania, the PA Rural Center reassessment report. For Indiana, Lincoln Institute working papers on circuit breakers and assessment reform.

**Pending legislation.** State legislature bill trackers. LegiScan covers all 50 states. For NY, the NY Senate's bill search. For MI, the legislature.mi.gov bill tracker.

**Reform precedents.** Earthrightsinstitute, Lincoln Institute, Strong Towns, the Center for Property Tax Reform. Track which cities adopted, which repealed, and which are mid-debate.

When the agent assembles a per-city brief, every numbered claim should cite a primary source (constitution article + section, statute citation, case citation) or a tier-one secondary source. Treat advocacy materials and op-eds as starting points but not as load-bearing citations.

## 9. Common traps and creative pathways

**Trap 1 — Treating uniformity clauses as monolithic.** Of 48 states with constitutional uniformity requirements, courts in fewer than five have interpreted them to require equal rates across all real property regardless of class. The text alone does not tell you the answer; the judicial gloss does.

**Trap 2 — Confusing classification authority with exemption authority.** A state may prohibit classifying land separately from improvements, yet allow exempting categories of property. The exemption pathway (Vehicle B) is independent of the classification pathway (Vehicle A) and routinely missed.

**Trap 3 — Assuming caps are absolute.** Almost every capped state has a voted-levy or excess-levy escape. The escape valve is the real constraint, not the headline cap.

**Trap 4 — Ignoring incorporation status.** In Maryland the difference between a chartered municipality (Rockville) and a county (Baltimore County) is the difference between Tier 1 and Tier 4. Always identify what statutory authority applies to *this exact entity*.

**Trap 5 — Treating Prop 13 as an assessment-quality problem.** California's barrier is not bad assessors; it's a constitutional rule that prevents parcel-level reassessment to current market value. No amount of GIS work fixes it. Distinguish "the data is messy" from "the law disallows the data."

**Trap 6 — Underweighting reversibility.** Statutory authority can be repealed (Amsterdam NY 1993 → repealed; Altoona PA 2011 → reverted 2016). Constitutional authority is more durable. The right pathway is sometimes the *slower* one with greater anti-repeal entrenchment.

**Trap 7 — Failing to enumerate every vehicle.** Always run the full Vehicle A–H menu before declaring a pathway blocked. Universal building exemption is the standout: it converts strict-uniformity states from "impossible" to "merely difficult."

**Trap 8 — Conflating residential/commercial classification with land/improvement classification.** Ohio, Massachusetts, Illinois Cook County, and others classify by *use* (Class I/II in Ohio, CIP in Mass, residential vs. commercial in Cook). This is Vehicle D, not Vehicles A–C. It captures different economics and should not be confused with split-rate.

**Creative pathway 1 — Voted-levy LVT.** Particularly powerful in WA (excess levies), IN (referendum levies), and any state with a robust voter-approval carve-out from levy caps. Underexplored.

**Creative pathway 2 — Building exemption with shadow rate.** Where the headline rate must remain uniform, exempt buildings universally and let the resulting effective rate calibration produce the shift. Politically marketable as "100% homestead exemption for the building you live in."

**Creative pathway 3 — Phase-in via expanding partial exemption.** Start at, e.g., 25% improvement exemption (within most legislatures' comfort), and ratchet up over a decade. Achieves transitional smoothing and political durability simultaneously.

**Creative pathway 4 — Pilot programs.** NY S7871 demonstrates the pilot model. Easier political ask than statewide change; useful for proof of concept in mid-sized cities.

**Creative pathway 5 — Special assessment district overlay.** Where general municipal authority is blocked, a special district's enabling statute may permit its own classification. Worth pursuing only if the city already has a strong special-district tradition.

## 10. Caveats

This guide synthesizes constitutional text, statutory citations, and secondary commentary as of May 2026. Legal authority changes — new legislation passes, courts re-interpret, voters amend. Before relying on any conclusion in a per-city brief for substantive action (drafting legislation, advising a city council), the underlying authorities should be re-verified with a licensed attorney admitted in the relevant state.

Nothing here is legal advice. The framework is a research tool: it organizes the questions a competent attorney will then answer. The point is to make the question-asking systematic so that no city is evaluated on a thinner record than another.

When in doubt, default to the more conservative reading of authority and require explicit primary-source citation before declaring a vehicle "open."
